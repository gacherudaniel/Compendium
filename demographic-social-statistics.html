1 Demographic and social statistics

1.1 Population

Population refers to the totality of all units of interest in a study or investigation at a given time in a given area.

1.1.1 Abortion

 Abortion is defined, with reference to the woman, as any interruption of pregnancy before 28 weeks of gestation with a dead foetus. There are two major categories of abortion: spontaneous and induced. Induced abortions are those initiated by deliberate action undertaken with the intention of terminating pregnancy; all other abortions are considered spontaneous.

1.1.2 Adoption

Adoption is the legal and voluntary taking and treating of the child of other parents as one’s own in so far as provided by the laws of the country. By means of a judicial process, whether related or not to the adopter, the adopted child acquires the rights and status of a legitimate child.

1.1.3 Age dependency ratio

Agedependency ratios give the number of economically inactive people in relation to each economically active person. Classifications of economically active and inactive are usually made on the basis of age, with everyone aged less than 15 and everyone aged 65 and over considered economically inactive. The overall dependency ratio can be broken down into constituent parts, giving the child dependency ratio (number of children in relation to each economically active person) and the old-age dependency ratio (number of people aged 65 and over in relation to each economically active person).

1.1.4 Age misreporting

This is the misreporting/mistating of a person’s age by giving a value which is less or more than actual, either intentionally or unintentionally.

1.1.5 Age ratio

It is the proportion of population in a given age group to its adjacent age groups, that is the successive and the preceding age groups.

1.1.6 Age

Age is the interval of time between the day, month and year of birth and the day, month and year of occurrence of the event expressed in the largest completed unit of solar time such as years for adults and children and months, weeks, days, hours or minutes of life, as appropriate, for infants under one year of age.

1.1.7 Age-sex accuracy index

It is an index that combines both the sex ratio and the age ratio for both males and females to generate a joint score. The score is used to evaluate the quality of age and sex data in population censuses or surveys.

1.1.8 Age-Specific Fertility Rate

 Refers to the annual number of births to women of a specified age or age group per 1,000 women in that age group.

1.1.9 Annulment of a marriage

The invalidation or voiding of a marriage by a competent authority, according to the laws of each country, which confers on the parties the status of never having been married to each other.

1.1.10 Base population

It is the starting or reference population on which population projections are based. In most cases the enumerated population is used as a base population.

1.1.11 Birth (or parity) order

Birth (or parity) order refers to the numerical order of the live birth or foetal death, recorded in relation to all previous pregnancy outcomes of the mother, irrespective of whether the outcome is a live birth or foetal death or whether pregnancies were nuptial or extranuptial.

1.1.12 Birth registration

Refers to the process of recording a child’s birth. It is a permanent and official record of a child’s existence and provides legal recognition of that child’s identity. It establishes a legal record of where the child was born and who the child’s parents are.

1.1.13 Burial permit

A burial permit is an official statement authorising the removal of the dead body (corpse) to the cemetery.

1.1.14 Child Headed Household

A household whose head and main decision maker or source of livelihood is below the age of 18 years.

1.1.15 Children Ever Born (CEB)

Refers to the life-time fertility experience of women 12 years and above. It is the total number of children a woman has given birth to in her lifetime.

1.1.16 Cohort

A cohort is a group of people who experienced the same demographic event during a particular period of time. This will most commonly refer to births during a set period of time, but may also refer to marriages, onset of puberty, migration, first birth etc.

1.1.17 Completeness of registration of a vital event

This is the proportion of vital events that are registered. It is the number of registered vital events divided by an estimate of the actual number of vital events that occurred in the same population during a specific period of time.

1.1.18 Content errors

These are errors in the characteristics of persons counted, resulting from incorrect reporting or recording or from failure to report.

1.1.19 Coverage errors

These are non-sampling errors that result from omitting a unit that should have been included, including a unit more than once, or including a unit that should not have been included.

1.1.20 Crude divorce rate

The crude divorce rate is the number of divorces occurring among the population of a given geographical area during a given year, per 1,000 mid-year total population of the given geographical area during the same year.

1.1.21 Crude marriage rate

The crude marriage rate is the number of marriages occurring among the population of a given geographical area during a given year, per 1,000 mid-year total population of the given geographical area during the same year.

1.1.22 Current Fertility

Refers to births that occurred to a woman in the 12 months before the census or survey date.

1.1.23 Date of birth

 The date of birth is the hour (minute), day, month and year of birth established in order that exact age may be determined in completed years, months, days, hours (minutes) of life as required.

1.1.24 De facto population

 De facto population consists of all persons who are physically present in the country or area of enumeration at the population and housing census enumeration reference date, whether or not they are usual residents.

1.1.25 De jure population

De jure population consists of all usual residents, whether or not they are present at the time of the population and housing census enumeration.

1.1.26 Death

Death is the permanent disappearance of all evidence of life at any time after the occurrence of live birth, i.e., the postnatal cessation of vital functions without capability of resuscitation. This definition excludes foetal deaths.

1.1.27 Defacto female headed household

Is the household where the husband is not present and the wife becomes the main decision maker in his absence during a survey or census data collection exercise.

1.1.28 Defacto Population Census

The collection of basic social, demographic and economic data for all people who will have spent a specified night in the reference area. A de facto census involves counting people who spent a specified night in a household or an institution.

1.1.29 Dejure female headed household

Is the household where the main decision maker is a female who has never been married or is divorced or is legally separated or widowed during a data collection exercise.

1.1.30 Dejure Population Census

The collection of basic social, demographic and economic data for all the people who normally reside in the country. It will exclude visitors from other countries and include residents who will be outside the country at the time of enumeration. At household or institution level the dejure census includes only the usual members. The problem with the dejure census is to define the “usual member” of a household or an institution.

1.1.31 Delayed Registration of a vital event

The registration of a vital event after the prescribed period denoted in existing laws, rules, or regulations (including any grace period, if one is specified). A late registration is the registration of a vital event after the prescribed time period but within a specified grace period. In Kenya, the grace period is from the third to sixth month following the event.

1.1.32 Demographic transition

The demographic transition is the process by which populations move from high mortality and fertility rates to low mortality and fertility rates as a country develops from a pre-industrial to an industrialized economic system. This is typically demonstrated through a demographic transition model. As fertility decline often lags behind mortality decline, there is an intermediate phase in the middle of the transition characterised by high growth rates.

1.1.33 Divorce

Divorce is the final legal dissolution of a marriage; a separation of husband and wife that confers on the parties the right to remarriage under civil, religious and/or other provisions in accordance with the laws of the country.

1.1.34 Dual events

Are those occurring simultaneously in the lives of two individuals, which cannot occur again in the life of either individual without a previous change to his or her status. Those events include marriage, registered partnership, separation, divorce, legal dissolution of registered partnerships and annulment of marriage.

1.1.35 Dual records system

A dual records system is the simultaneous collection of vital events, mainly births and deaths and the appropriate exposed-to-the-risk population, by two collecting methods which, ideally, are independent of each other:

a) a continuous vital event recording procedure which may be other than the civil registration system; and

b) a periodic household sample survey conducted in the same geographical area.

Matching of the events reported by the two procedures provides methods of improving the estimate of the total number of vital events.

1.1.36 Duration of marriage

The duration of a marriage refers to the interval of time between the day, month and year of marriage to date. It is often expressed in completed years.

1.1.37 Dwelling Unit

This is a place of abode or residence with a private entrance. There can be many dwelling units within a structure. A dwelling unit may have one or more habitable rooms.

1.1.38 Enumerator

A person who collects the required information from the enumeration area through interviews or any other method of obtaining information.

1.1.39 Family

A group of persons who are related by blood, marriage, or adoption and who usually live together in the same household.

1.1.40 Female Headed Household

A household whose primary decision-maker or source of livelihood (income) is a woman.

1.1.41 Fertility

Is the actual number of live births a woman has had by the end of her reproductive life span (15-49 years).

1.1.42 Foetal Death

Foetal Death is death prior to the complete expulsion or extraction from its mother of a product of conception, irrespective of the duration of the period of gestation. Death is indicated by the fact that after such separation, the foetus does not breathe or show any other evidence of life, such as beating of the heart, pulsation of the umbilical cord, or definite movement of voluntary muscles.

1.1.43 Habitable Rooms

Habitable rooms are defined as places/spaces in the dwelling unit that are used mainly for living and exclude stores, granaries, offices, toilets and garages.

1.1.44 Head of Household

Is the most responsible member of a household who makes key decisions of the household on a day-to-day basis and whose authority is recognized by all members of the household. It could be the father, mother or a child, or any other responsible member within the household.

1.1.45 Homestead

It is an isolated compound with one or more structures, and may be inhabited by one or more households. In most cases, fences, hedges, walls, etc. surround homesteads. A homestead may contain, for example, a hut or a group of huts. A manyatta, thus is considered as a homestead. However, a wall/fence or hedge need not necessarily surround a homestead. For instance, boys’ quarters, garage, kitchen, etc. may be part of a homestead whether or not they are surrounded by a fence/wall, etc.

1.1.46 Household Member

A person who normally lives in a household and is either present or temporarily away from the household for a period of less than six months at the time of enumeration. This excludes visitors.

1.1.47 Household

Is a person or a group of persons who reside in the same homestead or compound but not necessarily in the same dwelling unit. They have the same cooking arrangement and are answerable to the same household head.

1.1.48 Housing census

A housing census is the total process of planning, collecting, processing, analysing, disseminating and evaluating statistical data relating to the number and condition of housing units and facilities as available to the households pertaining, at a specified time, to all living quarters and occupants thereof in a country or in a well-delimited part of a country.

1.1.49 Illegitimate child

An illegitimate child is the child of parents who, according to national law or customs, were not married at the time of its birth.

1.1.50 Judicial (legal) separation

A judicial (legal) separation is the disunion of married persons according to the laws of each country, without conferring on the parties the right to remarriage.

1.1.51 Late Registration of a vital event

A late registration is the registration of a birth or death after the legally specified time period but within a specified grace period. The grace period is usually considered to be six months after the vital event.

1.1.52 Legitimation

The formal investing of a person with the status and rights of a person born in wedlock, according to the laws of each country.

1.1.53 Lifetime Fertility

Refers to the total number of children ever born alive during the entire reproductive period of the woman.

1.1.54 Live Birth

Live Birth is the complete expulsion or extraction from its mother of a product of conception, irrespective of the duration of pregnancy, which after such separation breathes or shows any other evidence of life such as beating of the heart, pulsation of the umbilical cord, or definite movement of voluntary muscles, whether or not the umbilical cord has been cut or the placenta is attached; each product of such a birth is considered live-born.

1.1.55 Male Headed Household

A household whose primary decision-maker or source of livelihood (income) is a man.

1.1.56 Marriage

An act, ceremony or process by which the legal relationship of spouses is constituted. The legality of the union may be established by civil, religious or other means as recognized by the laws of each country. Countries may wish to expand the definition to cover civil unions if they are registered. In that case, registered partnership usually refers to a legal construct, entailing registration with the public authorities according to the laws of each country, that becomes the basis for legal conjugal obligations between two persons.

Matching of the events reported by the two procedures provides methods of improving the estimate of the total number of vital events.

1.1.57 Medium-size Urban Centres

These are centres with a population of more than 10,000 but less than 100,000 people.

1.1.58 Nuptiality

Refers to the frequency of marriages between persons of opposite sexes which involve rights and obligations fixed by law or custom.

1.1.59 Parity

The total number of live births a woman has ever had. Women who have never had a live birth may be referred to as nulliparous. Women who have had one live birth are primiparous, and those with more than one live birth are multiparous.

1.1.60 Population Census

The total process of planning, collecting, processing, analysing, disseminating and evaluating demographic, economic and social data at the smallest geographic level pertaining, at a specified time, to all persons in a country or in a well-delimited part of a country.

1.1.61 Population Change

The increase or decrease of the total population or one of its structural units within a given time interval. The components of change in total population are births, deaths and migration.

1.1.62 Population Density

Population density is calculated as the total population divided by the total area which that population resides in. It provides a simple measure of the pressure of population on space and resources. Problems may arise when population density is calculated for countries with large areas which are uninhabitable, as it may give an impression of “emptiness” when actually the population is crowded in small areas within the total territory.

1.1.63 Population Distribution

The arrangement of the population in space at a given time, that is geographically or among the various types of residential areas.

1.1.64 Population Projection

It is an estimate of future population by considering changes in a population due to fertility (births), mortality (deaths) and migration.

1.1.65 Population Pyramid

A diagram showing the comparison of a human population by sex and age at a given time, consisting of a pair of histograms, one for each sex, laid horizontally in ascending order of age groups with a common base. The diagram is intended to provide a quick overall comparison of the age and sex structure of the population.

1.1.66 Population Size

The total number of persons/units in a specified area at a specified point in time

1.1.67 Population Structure

The distribution of the population by sex and age groups

1.1.68 Population/Demographic Momentum

Population momentum refers to the inbuilt potential for growth (or decline) inherent in a population’s age-sex structure. It is the amount a population would increase (or decrease) in size, if the fertility of the population changed immediately to replacement level.

1.1.69 Recognition

Is the legal acknowledgement, either voluntarily or compulsorily, of the paternity of a child born out of wedlock.

1.1.70 Respondent

A respondent is the person who provides answers to the interviewer or the person who records the answers in a self-administered questionnaire. This person should be mature and well informed about the unit of enumeration and its characteristics.

1.1.71 Response

An answer provided by the respondent during an interview/inquiry or the answer recorded by the respondent in a self-administered questionnaire.

1.1.72 Separation, judicial

The disunion of married persons, according to the laws of each country, without conferring on the parties the right to remarry.

1.1.73 Sex Ratio at Birth (SRB)

The number of males per 100 females at birth.

1.1.74 Sex Ratio

The number of males per 100 females in a population.

1.1.75 Singulate Mean Age at Marriage (SMAM)

Refers to the average number of years lived in the single state by those who ever marry before age 50.

1.1.76 Small Urban centres

These are centres with a population of less than 10,000 people.

1.1.77 Smoothed population

It is the population that has been adjusted for errors on age due to misreporting to give a more fitting demographic pattern.

1.1.78 Structure

A structure is a freestanding building used for the purposes of residential, business or any other activity.

1.1.79 Total fertility rate (TFR)

This is the average number of children born alive to a woman during her lifetime if she were to bear children at each age in accordance with the prevailing age-specific fertility rates. It is obtained by summing up all age-specific fertility rates for each year of the childbearing span.

1.1.80 Type of marriage

Type of marriage is the type of act, ceremony or process by which the legal relationship of husband and wife is constituted, that is, whether civil, religious or customary.

1.1.81 Usual Residence

The place at which the person has lived continuously for most of the last 12 months (that is, for at least six months or for at least the last 12 months, not including temporary absences for holidays or work assignments) or intends to live for at least six months.

1.1.82 Verbal Autopsy (VA)

VA is a process for diagnosing causes of death based on responses collected from families and/or caregivers to a series of structured questions on the signs and symptoms experienced by the deceased, and their duration. These responses are usually reviewed by a physician to determine the probable cause of death.

1.1.83 Vertical family events

Are those involving a descendant; they comprise adoption, legitimation and recognition.

1.1.84 Vital Event Record

A legal document entered in the civil register which attests to the occurrence and characteristics of a vital event.

1.1.85 Vital Events

The occurrence of a live birth, death, fetal death, marriage, divorce, adoption, legitimation, recognition of parenthood, annulment of marriage, or legal separation.

1.1.86 Vital statistical record

A vital statistical record is a document or form, containing the items of information concerning an individual vital event or a number of vital events of the same type, filled in at the time of registration, to meet the needs of vital statistics compilation.

1.1.87 Vital statistics system

In the context of defining a system as a set of interacting or independent components forming an integrating whole and for the purposes of these principles and recommendations, the vital statistics system’s components are (a) legal registration, (b) statistical reporting of, and (c) collection, compilation, and dissemination of statistics pertaining to vital events. The vital events of interest are live births, adoptions, legitimations, recognition; deaths and fetal deaths; and marriages, divorces, separations, and annulments of marriage.

1.1.88 Vital statistics

These constitute the collection of statistics on vital events in a lifetime of a person as well as relevant characteristics of the events themselves and of the person and persons concerned. Vital statistics provide crucial and critical information on the population in a country.

1.1.89 Youth Population

Refers to population age 15-34 for training and age 15-24 as used in enrolment for international comparison. In Kenya, youth are persons aged 18-34.

1.2 Migration

The movement of persons away from their place of usual residence, either across an international border or within a State.Must involve permanent change of usual residence.

1.2.1 An emigrant

A person who moves from his or her country of nationality or usual residence to another country, so that the country of destination effectively becomes his or her new country of usual residence from the perspective of the country of departure. Measures permanent exits from the reference country.

1.2.2 An immigrant

From the perspective of the country of arrival, a person who moves into a country other than that of his or her nationality or usual residence, so that the country of destination effectively becomes his or her new country of usual residence.Measures entries into a reference country that turn into permanent settlements.

1.2.3 An in-migrant

This is a person who enters a migration-defining area by crossing its boundary from some point outside the area, but within the same country.

1.2.4 An out-migrant

This is a person who departs from a migration-defining area by crossing its boundary to a point outside it, but within the same country.

1.2.5 Area of Origin: (Departure)

This is a place from which a migrant moves, whereas, area of destination (arrival) is the area to which a migrant moves.

1.2.6 Asylum seeker

An individual who is seeking international protection. In countries with individualized procedures, an asylum seeker is someone whose claim has not yet been finally decided on by the country in which he or she has submitted it. Not every asylum seeker will ultimately be recognized as a refugee, but every recognized refugee is initially an asylum seeker.

1.2.7 Country of origin

In the migration context, a country of nationality or of former habitual residence of a person or group of persons who have migrated abroad, irrespective of whether they migrate regularly or irregularly.

1.2.8 Foreign born

Are persons who were born in a country other than the country of enumeration where they currently reside. This group corresponds to the stock of international migrants that migrated at least once in their life and reside outside their country of birth.

1.2.9 Foreign-born citizens

Persons born abroad and having the citizenship of the country of residence. This group will in large part be formed of foreign-born immigrants who have received citizenship of the host country as well as persons with the citizenship of the host country who were born abroad.

1.2.10 Foreign-born foreigners

Are persons born abroad without the citizenship of the country of residence. This group will in large part be formed by foreign-born immigrants who did not acquire citizenship of the host country.

1.2.11 Identity document

An official piece of documentation issued by the competent authority of a State designed to prove the identity of the person carrying it.

1.2.12 Immigration

From the perspective of the country of arrival, the act of moving into a country other than one’s country of nationality or usual residence, so that the country of destination effectively becomes his or her new country of usual residence.

1.2.13 Internal migrant

Any person who is moving or has moved within a State for the purpose of establishing a new temporary or permanent residence or because of displacement.

1.2.14 Internal Migration

This refers to migration across regional administrative boundaries within a country. Internal migration can be categorized by type (in-migration and out-migration) and directional flow (rural-urban, rural-rural, urban-rural, and urban-urban).

1.2.15 Internally displaced persons

Persons or groups of persons who have been forced or obliged to flee or to leave their homes or places of habitual residence, in particular as a result of or in order to avoid the effects of armed conflict, situations of generalized violence, violations of human rights or natural or human-made disasters, and who have not crossed an internationally recognized State border.

1.2.16 International migration

Is the process by which one changes his place of usual residence by crossing international boundaries into another country.

1.2.17 Labour migration

Movement of persons from one State to another, or within their own country of residence, for the purpose of employment.

1.2.18 Migrant

An umbrella term, not defined under international law, reflecting the common lay understanding of a person who moves away from his or her place of usual residence, whether within a country or across an international border, temporarily or permanently, and for a variety of reasons.

1.2.19 Migrant worker

A person who is to be engaged, is engaged or has been engaged in a remunerated activity in a State of which he or she is not a national.

1.2.20 Migration stocks and flows

The stock of international migrants is the total number of international migrants living in a country at a particular point in time, while the flow of international migrants is the number of migrants entering or leaving a country over the course of a specific time period (e.g. one year). Country of birth and citizenship are the main criteria used for categorizing different types of population stocks and flows, with duration of stay providing a further element for statistics on migration flows.

1.2.21 Nativity status

Refers to whether a person is native-born or foreign-born in relation to a specific country.

1.2.22 Nationality

The legal bond between an individual and a State.

1.2.23 Native-born citizens

Persons born in the country of residence, with the citizenship of the country of residence. This group will be in large part formed of the native born who are citizens of the country. It will also include descendants of the foreign born who were born in the country and received citizenship of the country of birth, at the time of birth or later.

1.2.24 Native-born foreigners

Persons born in the country of residence without having the citizenship of the country. This group will in large part be formed by descendants of the foreign born who did not receive citizenship of the host country at birth.

1.2.25 Naturalization

Any mode of acquisition after birth of a nationality not previously held by the person that requires an application by this person or his or her legal agent as well as an act of granting nationality by a public authority.

1.2.26 Net-Migration

This is the balance between in-migration and out-migration. According to direction of the balance, it may be characterized as net in-migration or net out-migration. Thus, net flow in or out is indicated by a plus (+) or minus (-) sign.

1.2.27 Passport

A document issued by the competent authority of a State, valid for international travels, which identifies the holder as a national of the issuing State and constitutes evidence of the holder’s right to return to that State.

1.2.28 Permanent resident

A non-national with the right of permanent residence in a State of destination.

1.2.29 Recent Migration

This is movement in the recent past of persons enumerated during the census, relative to their previous place of residence a year prior to census. A recent migrant is one whose current area of residence is different from his or her previous place of residence, one year ago. Note that if the person was still living in the county then he/she was not considered as a migrant.

1.2.30 Refugee (1951 Convention)

A person who, owing to a well-founded fear of persecution for reasons of race, religion, nationality, membership of a particular social group or political opinion, is outside the country of his nationality and is unable or, owing to such fear, is unwilling to avail himself of the protection of that country; or who, not having a nationality and being outside the country of his former habitual residence as a result of such events, is unable or, owing to such fear, is unwilling to return to it.

1.2.31 Repatriation

The personal right of a prisoner of war, civil detainee, refugee, or of a civilian to return to his or her country of nationality under specific conditions laid down in various international instruments.

1.2.32 Return Migration

Return migration occurs when a return migrant (person) moves back to the area where he or she formerly resided.

1.2.33 Sexual exploitation

Any actual or attempted abuse of a position of vulnerability, differential power, or trust, for sexual purposes, including, but not limited to, profiting monetarily, socially, or politically from the sexual exploitation of another.

1.2.34 Sexual violence

Sexual violence is a form of gender-based violence and encompasses any sexual act, attempt to obtain a sexual act, unwanted sexual comments or advances, or acts to traffic, or otherwise directed against a person’s sexuality using coercion, by any person regardless of their relationship to the victim, in any setting. Sexual violence takes multiple forms and includes rape, sexual abuse, forced pregnancy, forced sterilization, forced abortion, forced prostitution, trafficking, sexual enslavement, forced circumcision, castration and forced nudity.

1.2.35 Short-term international migrants

These are international migrants who move to a country for a period of at least 3 months but less than one year.

1.2.36 Short-term Migrant

A person who changes his or her place of usual residence for more than three months but less than a year (12 months). Except in cases where the movement to that country is for purposes of recreation, holiday, visits to friends or relatives, business or medical treatment.

1.2.37 State of Origin

The State of which the person concerned is a national.

1.2.38 Stateless Person

A person who is not considered as a national by any State under the operation of its law.

1.2.39 Statelessness

The condition of not being considered as a national by any State under the operations of its law.

1.2.40 Travel Document

A document issued by a government or by an international organization which is accepted as a proof of identity for the purpose of crossing international borders.

1.2.41 Treaty

An international agreement concluded between States in written form and governed by international law, whether embodied in a single instrument or in two or more related instruments and whatever its particular designation.

1.2.42 Undocumented migrant

A non-national who enters or stays in a country without the appropriate documentation.

1.3 Labour

1.3.1 Core Labour Force Concepts & Status

1.3.1.1 Industry

A group of economic producing units (establishments or enterprises) that are primarily engaged in the same or similar kinds of productive activity.

1.3.1.2 Job

A set of tasks and duties performed or meant to be performed by one person for a single economic unit.

1.3.1.3 Occupation

A set of similar tasks or duties performed by a person in employment.

1.3.1.4 Work

Any activity (irrespective of legality, formal/informal nature, and consistent with the scope of productive activities within the SNA general production boundary) performed by a person of any sex and age to produce goods or provide services for use by others or for own use.

1.3.1.5 Employment (Work for pay or profit)

Work performed for others in exchange for pay (in cash or in kind) or profit. This is the traditional focus of labour force statistics. e.g. Employees (permanent, temporary, casual), self-employed individuals (employers, own-account workers), contributing family workers who receive direct pay or a share of profits.

1.3.1.6 Own-use Production Work

Work performed to produce goods or provide services mainly or exclusively for the household’s or family’s own final use. This activity must satisfy the “third-person criterion” (i.e., it must be possible for someone else to perform the activity on one’s behalf). e.g.

a)     Production of goods: Growing food for household consumption (subsistence farming), fetching water, collecting firewood, constructing or maintaining one’s own dwelling, manufacturing goods for household use (e.g., clothes, furniture) and

b)    Provision of services: Childcare, care for dependent adults, cooking, cleaning, and other domestic tasks within one’s own household.

1.3.1.7 Unpaid Trainee Work

Work performed for others without pay, primarily to acquire workplace experience or skills. e.g. Unpaid internships, apprenticeships (where no pay or significant remuneration is received beyond perhaps a small stipend for expenses), or traineeships.

1.3.1.8 Volunteer Work

Non-compulsory work performed for others without pay, driven by a desire to help others, contribute to communities, the environment, or support organizations. e.g. Volunteering for a charity, community service, helping a neighbour without payment, environmental clean-up efforts.

1.3.1.9 Other Work Activities

A residual category for work activities that do not fit into the other four forms. This can include unpaid compulsory work (e.g., community service as a legal requirement, work for traditional communal obligations). e.g. prison labour (if unpaid)

1.3.1.10 Working Age Population

All persons aged 15 years and older. This is the potential pool of labour. It includes individuals who are employed, unemployed, or outside the labour force (e.g., students, homemakers, retirees).

1.3.1.11 Employed Persons

All individuals of working age who, during a short reference period (typically one week), either worked for pay or profit for at least one hour, or were temporarily absent from a job in which they had already worked, due to reasons like sick leave, vacation, or maternity/paternity leave.

1.3.1.12 Unemployed Persons

All working-age persons who were not in employment, carried out activities to seek employment during a specified recent period and were currently available to take up employment given a job opportunity. Note: individuals engaged in unpaid or own-use production work (like subsistence farming) are not considered unemployed, even if they want paid work. Instead, they may fall outside the labour force or potential labour force categories

1.3.1.13 Labour Force

This comprises all persons of working age who furnish the supply of labour for the production of goods and services during a specified time-reference period. It refers to the sum of all persons of working age who are employed and those who are unemployed.

1.3.1.14 Employment-to-Population Ratio

It is the proportion of a country’s working-age population that is employed.

1.3.1.15 Labour Force Participation Rate

The labour force participation rate is the proportion of the working-age population that is in the labour force.

1.3.1.16 Persons Outside the Labour Force

Thisincludes working-age persons neither in employment nor in unemployment.

1.3.1.17 Future Starter

A person “not in employment” and “currently available” who does not “seek employment” because he or she has already made arrangements to start a job within a short subsequent period, set according to the general length of waiting time for starting a new job in the national context but generally not greater than three months.

1.3.1.18 Labour Underutilization

Thisrefers to mismatches between labour supply and demand, which translate into an unmet need for employment among the population. There are several measures of labour underutilization including unemployment, time-related underemployment, the potential labour force, and the LU1-LU4 rates.

1.3.1.19 Time-related Underemployment

This is defined as all persons in employment who wanted to work additional hours, whose working time in all jobs was less than a specified hours threshold, and who were available to work additional hours given an opportunity for more work.

1.3.1.20 Potential Labour Force

This is a subset of persons outside the labour force, which aims to measure the potential supply of labour, or in other words, it quantifies all persons with an unmet need for income-generating work and provides a measure of their degree of labour market integration. It includes persons who are either looking for a job but not currently available to work, or who are available to work but are not actively looking for a job.

1.3.1.21 Extended Labour Force

This is defined as the sum of the potential labour force and the labour force. It includes persons of working age who, during the short reference period, were neither in employment nor in unemployment and: (a) carried out activities to “seek employment”, were not “currently available” but would become available within a short subsequent period (i.e. unavailable jobseekers); or (b) did not carry out activities to “seek employment”, but wanted employment and were “currently available” (i.e. available potential jobseekers).

1.3.1.22 Unemployment

This includes all working-age persons who were not in employment, carried out activities to seek employment during a specified recent period and were currently available to take up employment given a job opportunity.

1.3.1.23 Unemployment Rate (LU1)

This is the share of the labour force that is unemployed.

1.3.1.24 Combined rate of time-related underemployment and unemployment (LU2)

This represents the share of the labour force that is either in unemployment or time-related underemployment.

1.3.1.25 Combined rate of unemployment and potential labour force (LU3)

This represents the share of the extended labour force that is in unemployment or the potential labour force.

1.3.1.26 Composite measure of labour underutilization (LU4)

This represents the share of the extended labour force that is in unemployment, time-related underemployment, or the potential labour force.

1.3.1.27 Long Term Unemployment

This is a situation of the unemployed population where the duration of employment search is 12 months or more, including the reference period.

1.3.1.28 Seasonal Unemployment

Unemployment varies predictably with the season. Cyclical unemployment is associated with a reduction in real output and occurs during a recession.

1.3.1.29 Formal Sector Enterprises

Formal sector enterprises are economic units that are formally constituted, recognized, and regulated by government authorities. They operate within the scope of national legal and regulatory frameworks, including those related to taxation, business registration, labour laws, and social security. Their primary objective is typically the production of goods and services for the market to generate income or profit, or for non-profit purposes, or non-market production for government/public use.

1.3.1.30 Informal Sector Enterprises

Informal sector enterprises are unincorporated household enterprises that are not formally constituted, recognized, or regulated by government authorities, or are not sufficiently covered by formal arrangements in law or in practice. Their production of goods and services is primarily intended for the market with the purpose of generating income and profit, but they operate outside or insufficiently within formal legal and regulatory frameworks.

1.3.1.31 Household Sector

This refers to households acting as employers for individuals performing domestic services (e.g., housekeepers, nannies, gardeners) for remuneration. The employment relationship for these domestic workers can be either formal or informal. The “household” in this context is the employer unit, not an “enterprise” in the typical business sense, nor is the work itself usually considered “own-use production” as it’s for remuneration.

1.3.2 Types of Work & Employment Relationships

1.3.2.1 Full-time Work

Typically involves working a standard number of hours per week (e.g., 35-40 hours), with a regular schedule and consistent pay. Often comes with benefits like health insurance, paid leave, and retirement plans.

1.3.2.2 Part-time Work

This involves working fewer hours than full-time employees, often with flexible schedules. Pay is usually on a pro-rata basis, and benefits may or may not be included.

1.3.2.3 Casual Work/Employment

Highly flexible, “as-needed” basis, without guaranteed hours or long-term commitment. Payment is often daily or per task, and typically without standard benefits. It does not provideengagements for a period longer than 90 days. They have no formal employment contract with the employer and their services may be terminated with or without notice.

1.3.2.4 Temporary Employment

Work for a limited period, often project-based, seasonal, or to cover absences. May be direct hires or through staffing agencies.

1.3.2.5 Contract Employment (Fixed-Term)

Hired for a specific project, task, or defined timeframe, with a start and end date specified in the contract. Often treated as self-employed for tax purposes.

1.3.2.6 Seasonal Employment

Work opportunities available only during specific times of the year when demand for goods or services peaks (e.g., agriculture, tourism, retail during holidays).

1.3.2.7 Freelance / Self-Employment (Independent Contractor)

Individuals who work for themselves, offering services to multiple clients or operating their own business. They control their own work, manage their taxes and benefits, and are not employees of a company.

1.3.2.8 Internship

Short-term work or training opportunities designed for students or new graduates to gain practical experience, often for little or no pay, or for academic credit.

1.3.2.9 Apprenticeship

A structured training program that combines on-the-job learning with formal education or classroom instruction, typically for skilled trades. Apprentices earn a salary while learning.

1.3.2.10 Gig Work / On-Demand Work

Short-term, task-based jobs are often facilitated through digital platforms (e.g., ride-sharing, food delivery, online freelance tasks). Characterized by flexibility but often lacks traditional employee benefits and job security.

1.3.2.11 Formal Employment

Formal employment refers to work arrangements that are recognized, regulated, and protected by existing legal and regulatory frameworks. Workers in formal employment typically have explicit (written or oral) contracts, defined working conditions, and access to social protection and benefits. Primarily occurs within formal sector enterprises (businesses and organizations that are registered with tax authorities, operate under legal frameworks, and adhere to regulations).

1.3.2.12 Informal Employment

Informal employment refers to all remunerative work (both self-employment and wage employment) that is not registered, regulated, or protected by existing legal or regulatory frameworks, and therefore lacks access to social protection and employment benefits. It also includes non-remunerative work undertaken in an income-producing enterprise (e.g., contributing family workers who do not receive formal pay).

1.3.3 Employment Status by Type of Authority

1.3.3.1 Independent Workers

These workers generally own and control the economic unit in which they work, make important decisions, and are not dependent on a single other economic unit for market access.

1.3.4 Employers

1.3.4.1 Employers in corporations

Individuals who own and control incorporated businesses and employ one or more persons on a continuous basis.

1.3.4.2 Employers in household market enterprises

Individuals who own and control unincorporated businesses (household enterprises) and employ one or more persons on a continuous basis.

1.3.5 Independent workers without employees

1.3.5.1 Owner-operators of corporations without employees

Individuals who own and operate incorporated businesses but do not employ any employees on a continuous basis (they might be the sole worker or work with contributing family members).

1.3.5.2 Own-account workers in household market enterprises without employees

Individuals who operate unincorporated household market enterprises without employing any employees on a continuous basis. This is the traditional “self-employed” category.

1.3.5.3 Dependent Workers

These workers typically provide labour for another economic unit or person, with less control over their work organization or exposure to economic risk compared to independent workers.

1.3.5.4 Dependent contractors

This is a new and significant category. It refers to workers who provide labour to others under contractual arrangements that are similar to self-employment, but they are dependent on a single or a limited number of economic units for the organization of their work, income, or access to the market. They often do not employ others and are sometimes referred to as “dependent self-employed” or “false self-employed.”

1.3.6 Employees

1.3.6.1 Permanent Employees

Employees whose contracts are of indefinite duration or for a very long or unlimited period.

1.3.6.2 Fixed-term Employees

Employees whose contracts are for a specific, limited duration, after which they are expected to terminate.

1.3.6.3 Short-term and Casual Employees

Employees whose contracts are for a very short, often intermittent period, or on an “as needed” basis, typically without benefits associated with more stable employment.

1.3.6.4 Paid Apprentices, Trainees and Interns

Individuals undergoing structured training programs for a specific occupation, receiving remuneration for their work.

1.3.6.5 Contributing Family Workers

Individuals who hold a self-employment job in a market-oriented establishment operated by a related person living in the same household, and who are not regarded as partners. They typically receive no direct pay in the form of a wage or salary.

1.3.7 Hours and Wages

1.3.7.1 Actual Hours of Work

a)     This includes:

b)    Hours actually worked during normal periods of work;

c)     Time worked in addition to hours actually worked during normal periods of work, and generally paid at higher than normal rates (overtime);

d)    Time spent at the workplace on activities such as preparation of a workplace, repair and maintenance, preparation and cleaning of tools, and preparation of receipts, time sheets, and reports;

e)     Time spent at a workplace waiting or standing-by for such reasons as lack, breakdown of machinery, or accidents or time spent at place of work during which no work is done but for which payment is made under a guaranteed employment contract; and

f)      Time corresponding to short rest periods at the workplace, including tea and coffee breaks.

1.3.7.2 Normal Hours of Work

Hours worked or to be worked as prescribed by an employment contract.

1.3.7.3 Usual Hours of Work

Hours worked in a typical period (usually a week or a day) in an activity. It applies both to persons at work and to persons temporarily absent from work.

1.3.7.4 Wage Bill

A sum total of money paid to all employees for the labour rendered in a specified period.

1.3.7.5 Earnings

Regular remuneration received by employees either in cash and/or in kind. These include direct wages and salaries for time worked or work done, remuneration for time not worked (for example, paid annual leave), as well as bonuses and gratuities regularly received.

1.3.7.6 Nominal Wage

The total in-cash or in-kind remuneration before adjustment for inflation payable to the employee by the enterprise for the work performed by the employee during the accounting period.

1.3.7.7 Real Wage

The total in-cash or in-kind remuneration after adjustment for inflation payable to the employee by the enterprise for the work performed by the employee during the accounting period.

1.3.7.8 Minimum Wage

The lowest level of wage rate permitted, which is enforceable under threat of penal or other appropriate sanctions.

1.3.8 Labour Market Dynamics & Issues

1.3.8.1 Labour Input

Defined and measured as the number of employed persons or total employment.

1.3.8.2 Labour Productivity

The total volume of output (measured in terms of GDP) achieved per unit of labour (measured in terms of employed persons).

1.3.8.3 Underemployment

The type of employment comprising all persons in paid or self-employment, whether at work or not at work, involuntarily working less than normal duration of work determined for the activity, and were seeking or available for additional work during the reference period.

1.3.8.4 Working Poor

The number of employed persons living below a poverty level determined by a national/international authority.

1.3.8.5 Economic Status

Refers to the economic well-being of an individual.

1.3.9 Child Labour & Youth

1.3.9.1 Child Work

Production activity undertaken by children, whether for the market or not, paid or unpaid, part-time or full-time, on a casual basis or a regular one, in the formal sector or the informal, whether the activity is legal or illegal. It also includes work in a family enterprise and in household-based production activity, as well as domestic work in another household for at least an hour.

1.3.9.2 Child Labour

It is defined by its consequences as work that:

a)     is mentally, physically, and socially dangerous and harmful to children; and

b)    interferes with their schooling by depriving them of the opportunity to attend school; obliging them to leave school prematurely; and requiring them to attempt to combine school attendance with excessively long hours and heavy work.

1.3.9.3 Worst Forms of Child Labour

It is defined as:

a)     Forms of slavery or practices similar to slavery, such as the sale and trafficking of children, debt bondage and serfdom, as well as forced or compulsory labour, including forced or compulsory recruitment of children for use in armed conflict;

b)    The use of, procuring, or offering of a child for prostitution, for the production of pornography, or pornographic performances; the use, procurement, or offering of a child for illicit activities, in particular for the production and the trafficking of drugs as defined in relevant international treaties; and

c)     Work which, by its nature or the circumstances in which it is carried out, is likely to harm the health, safety, or morals of children, such harmful work to be determined by national authority.

1.3.9.4 Youth Unemployment

Refers to a labour market situation in which young people (of a specified age used to define youth) not in employment are seeking work and are available for work.

1.3.9.5 Youth Not in Education and not in Employment (NEET)

The percentage of youth who are not in employment and not in education or training.

1.3.10 Industrial Relations & Associations

1.3.10.1 Trade Union

An organisation whose membership consists of workers and union leaders to protect and promote their common interest, and whose principal purpose is to:

1.       Negotiate wages and working conditions;

2.     Regulate relationships between the workers and the employers;

3.      Take collective action to enforce the terms of collective bargaining;

4.     Raise new demands on behalf of their members and help settle their grievances.

1.3.10.2 Trade Union Density or Union Membership Rate

The ratio of the number of employees who are members of trade unions to all the employees in a country or population.

1.3.10.3 Collective Bargaining

A good-faith process between an organisation’s management and a trade union representing its members for negotiating wages, working hours, working conditions, and other matters of mutual interest. Collective bargaining is the fundamental principle on which the trade union system is based.

1.3.10.4 Strike

The cessation of work or withdrawal of labour by a body of persons employed in any combination/concerted refusal or refusal under a common understanding of any number of employees to continue working or provide labour.

1.4 Education

The processes by which societies deliberately transmit their accumulated information, knowledge, understanding, attitudes, values, skills, competencies and behaviours across generations. It involves communication designed to bring about learning.

The terms defined below are used in computing statistics and indicators on education and training in Kenya

1.4.1 Academic year

The annual teaching or examination period during which students attend courses or take final examinations, not taking minor breaks into account. It may be shorter than 12 months but would typically not be shorter than 9 months. It may vary for different levels of education or types of educational institutions within a country. This is also referred to as the ‘school year’, mainly for the pretertiary level.

1.4.2 Adjusted Net Enrolment Rate, Primary

Number of pupils of the school-age group for primary education, enrolled either in primary or secondary education expressed as a percentage of the total population in that age group.

1.4.3 Adjusted Net Enrolment Rate, Secondary

Number of students of the school-age group for secondary education, enrolled either in secondary or post-secondary education expressed as a percentage of the total population in that age group.

1.4.4 Adult education

Education is specifically targeted at individuals who are regarded as adults by their society to improve their technical or professional qualifications, further develop their abilities, enrich their knowledge with the purpose to complete a level of formal education, or to acquire, refresh or update their knowledge, skills and competencies in a particular field. This also includes what may be referred to as ‘continuing education’, ‘recurrent education’ or ‘second chance education’.

1.4.5 Adult Literacy

Persons above 18 years who can read and write meaningfully with understanding in any language.

1.4.6 Age-Specific Enrolment Rate (ASER)

Enrollment of a specific single age enrolled, irrespective of the level of education, as a percentage of the population of the same age.

1.4.7 Assessment of learning outcomes

Evaluation of an individual’s achievement of learning objectives, using a variety of assessment methods (written, oral and practical tests/examinations, projects and portfolios) during or at the end of an education programme.

1.4.8 Basic Literacy

This is a program designed to impart basic skills to learners and refers to adult education classes.

1.4.9 Bachelor’s or equivalent level

Often designed to provide participants with intermediate academic and/or professional knowledge, skills and competencies, leading to a first degree or equivalent qualification. Programmes at this level are typically theoretically-based but may include practical components and are informed by state of the art research and/or best professional practice. They are traditionally offered by universities and equivalent tertiary educational institutions.

1.4.10 Children Over-Age for Grade (Primary Education, Secondary Education)

Percentage of pupils in each level of education (primary and secondary education) who are at least 2 years above the intended age for their grade.

1.4.11 Communication

A relationship between two or more persons or an inanimate medium and persons, involving the transfer of information (messages, ideas, knowledge, strategies, etc.). Communication may be verbal or non-verbal, direct/face-to-face or indirect/remote, and may involve a wide variety of channels and media.

1.4.12 Completion of an education programme

Participation in all components of an education programme (including final examinations if any), irrespective of the result of any potential assessment of achievement of learning objectives.

1.4.13 Completion Rate

Percentage of a cohort of children or young people aged 3-5 years above the intended age for the last grade of each level of education who have completed that grade. It is a measurement of delay in the completion of a level of education.

1.4.14 Competency-Based Curriculum (CBC)

Covers all levels of basic education from pre-primary education and includes university education. The new system comprises 2 years of pre-primary education, 6 years of primary education (3 years lower primary and 3 years upper primary), 6 years of secondary education (3 years junior secondary and 3 years senior secondary) and 3 years of university education.

The official school-going age for pre-primary education under CBC is age 4-5 years while that of primary education is age 6-11 years, secondary education is 12-17 years and 18 years and above for university education.

1.4.15 Course

A unit of instruction comprising a sequence of educational activities in a particular field or range of related fields of education. This can also be referred to as a ‘module’, ‘unit’ or ‘subject’.

1.4.16 Credit

Unit in which the successful completion of courses or modules is earned and documented during and at the end of an education programme. Credits express the volume of learning based on a typical workload needed to achieve the expected learning objectives.

1.4.17 Degree

Educational qualification awarded upon successful completion of specific education programmes in tertiary education (traditionally by universities or equivalent institutions).

1.4.18 Doctoral or equivalent level

Are designed primarily to lead to an advanced research qualification. Programmes at this ISCED level are devoted to advanced study and original research and are typically offered only by research-oriented tertiary educational institutions such as universities. Doctoral programmes exist in both academic and professional fields.

1.4.19 Dropout Rate

Proportion of pupils from a cohort enrolled in a given grade at a given school year who are no longer enrolled in the following school year. The analysis of the 2019 Census data has computed the dropout rate as the proportion of the population that left school before completion to those who ever attended school before completion of the primary or secondary education cycle.

1.4.20 Dual-system education programmes

Programmes that combine school- or college- and work- based education. Both components are substantial (i.e. go beyond a single internship or occasional class), although the work-based part usually occupies 50% of the programme time or more.

1.4.21 Early Childhood Development and Education (ECDE)

Provides pre-primary schooling for children of age 4-5 years under the level of Early Years of Education. Early childhood education is typically designed with a holistic approach to support children’s early cognitive, physical, social and emotional development and introduce young children to organized instruction outside of the family context.

1.4.22 Education programme

A coherent set or sequence of educational activities designed and organized to achieve pre-determined learning objectives or accomplish a specific set of educational tasks over a sustained period. Within an education programme, educational activities may also be grouped into sub-components variously described in national contexts as ‘courses’, ‘modules’, ‘units’ and/or ‘subjects’. A programme may have major components not normally characterised as courses, units or modules – for example, play-based activities, periods of work experience, research projects and the preparation of dissertations.

1.4.23 Education provider

An organization that provides education, either as a main or ancillary objective. This can be a public educational institution, as well as a private enterprise, non-governmental organization or non-educational public body.

1.4.24 Education System

The overall network of institutions and programmes through which education of all types and/or all levels is provided to the population.

1.4.25 Educational activity

Deliberate activity involving some form of communication intended to bring about learning.

1.4.26 Educational attainment

Is the highest ISCED (International Standard Classification of Education) level successfully completed by an individual.

1.4.27 Educational institution

Established institution that provides education as its main purpose, such as a school, college, university or training centre. Such institutions are normally accredited or sanctioned by the relevant national education authorities or equivalent authorities. Educational institutions may also be operated by private organizations, such as religious bodies, special interest groups or private educational and training enterprises, both for profit and non-profit.

1.4.28 Educational Level

The highest level of formal training attained by an individual. In Kenya there are three levels of formal education namely primary, secondary and tertiary.

1.4.29 Enrolment

Individuals officially registered in a given education programme, or stage or module thereof, regardless of age.

1.4.30 Formal Education

Education that is institutionalized, intentional and planned through public organizations and recognized private bodies and in their totality constitute the formal education system of a country. Formal education consists mostly of initial education and in Kenya, it is offered at pre-primary, primary, secondary to university (including middle-level colleges) and it is normally provided as a continuous ‘ladder’ of full-time education.

1.4.31 Formal School

It is a structured educational institution that follows a standardized curriculum, has defined policies and procedures, and is typically recognized and regulated by government authorities. This school has a clear organizational structure, qualified teachers, and facilities dedicated to teaching and learning. Learners attending a formal school will usually progress through grades or levels, leading to recognized qualifications or certifications upon completion.

1.4.32 General education

Education programmes that are designed to develop learners’ general knowledge, skills and competencies, as well as literacy and numeracy skills, often to prepare participants for more advanced education programmes at the same or a higher ISCED level and to lay the foundation for lifelong learning. These programmes are typically school- or college-based. General education includes education programmes that are designed to prepare participants for entry into vocational education but do not prepare for employment in a particular occupation, trade or class of occupations or trades, nor lead directly to a labour market-relevant qualification.

1.4.33 Grade

A specific stage of instruction in initial education usually covered during an academic year. Students in the same grade are usually of similar age. This is also referred to as a ‘class’, ‘cohort’ or ‘year’. School attendance is regular attendance at any regular accredited educational institution or programme, public or private, for organized learning at any level of education.

1.4.34 Graduates

These are those who entered and successfully completed an education programme which is classified as ‘level completion’.

1.4.35 Gross Enrolment Rate (GER)

Total enrollment in a specific level of education, regardless of age, is expressed as a percentage of the eligible official school-age population corresponding to the same level of education in a given school year.

1.4.36 Gross Intake Ratio to the Last Grade (Primary, Secondary)

Total number of new entrants into the last grade of primary or secondary education, regardless of age, expressed as a percentage of the population at the intended entrance age to the last grade of primary education or secondary education.

1.4.37 Highest Level of Education Reached or Completed

The highest grade or level of education which the population 4 years and above have reached or attained.

1.4.38 Incidental or random learning

Occur as a by-product of day-to-day activities, events or communication that are not designed as deliberate educational or learning activities. Examples may include learning that takes place during the course of a meeting, whilst listening to a radio programme, or watching a television broadcast that is not designed as an education programme.

1.4.39 Informal learning

Forms of learning that are intentional or deliberate but are not institutionalised. It is consequently less organized and structured than either formal or non-formal education. Informal learning may include learning activities that occur in the family, workplace, local community and daily life, on a self-directed, family-directed or socially directed basis.

1.4.40 Informal School

Is an educational setting that provides education in non-traditional settings. This school often offers flexible schedules, personalized learning approaches, and may focus on specific interests or skills. An informal school may not adhere to strict curriculum guidelines or educational standards established by government authorities, but they provide valuable educational opportunities tailored to the needs of individual learners. Examples of Informal schools are community-based learning centers, homeschooling arrangements, or alternative education programs that provide education in non-traditional settings.

1.4.41 Integrated Schools (Regular and special needs)

These are schools that have inclusive educational settings where Learners with special needs are included in regular classrooms alongside their typically developing peers. These schools support and accommodate learners with special needs and disabilities to enable them to participate fully in schooling. The schools promote social integration and provide a supportive environment for all learners.

1.4.42 International Curriculum

Schools that offer educational programs based on curricula from other countries (e.g., British-IGCSE, American, International Baccalaureate, etc).

1.4.43 Kenya Competency Based Curriculum (KCBC)/National Curriculum

System of education in which learners are imparted with the ability to apply appropriate knowledge and skills to successfully perform a function. The curriculum is designed to emphasize the importance of not only developing skills and knowledge but also applying these to real life situations. The integration of pertinent and contemporary issues and service learning into the framework will provide the opportunity for learners to develop and apply their skills and knowledge, or in other words, their competencies.

1.4.44 Learning

An individual acquisition or modification of information, knowledge, understanding, attitudes, values, skills, competencies or behaviours through experience, practice, study or instruction.

1.4.45 Learning activity

Deliberate activity in which an individual participates with the intention to learn.

1.4.46 Learning objectives

Specification of learning outcomes to be achieved upon completion of an educational or learning activity. These encompass improving knowledge, skills and competencies within any personal, civic, social or employment-related context. Learning objectives are typically linked to the purpose of preparing for more advanced studies and/or an occupation, trade, or class of occupations or trades.

1.4.47 Learning outcomes

The totality of information, knowledge, understanding, attitudes, values, skills, competencies or behaviours an individual is expected to master upon successful completion of an education programme.

1.4.48 A learner

Is a person of either sex, not classified as usually economically active, who attends any regular educational institution, public or private, for systematic instruction at any level of education.

1.4.49 Literacy

The ability to write meaningfully and read with understanding in any language. A person who cannot write meaningfully and read with understanding in any language is considered illiterate. A literate person is one who can both read and write, with understanding, a short, simple statement on his or her everyday life. An illiterate person is one who cannot, with understanding, both read and write such a statement.

1.4.50 Literacy rate

The proportion of the population above 10 years who can write and read with understanding, expressed as a percentage of the total population above that age.

1.4.51 Madrassa

Is a pre-school that provides learning to children on koranic education and basic literacy.

1.4.52 Master’s or equivalent level

Are often designed to provide participants with advanced academic and/or professional knowledge, skills and competencies, leading to a second degree or equivalent qualification. Programmes at this level may have a substantial research component but do not yet lead to the award of a doctoral qualification. Typically, programmes at this level are theoretically-based but may include practical components and are informed by state of the art research and/or best professional practice. They are traditionally offered by universities and other tertiary educational institutions.

1.4.53 Middle-Level Colleges Education

Which is part of TVET education offers business and professional studies programmes leading to the award of certificates and diplomas in various disciplines. These learning institutions are open to students who have completed secondary education.

1.4.54 Net Enrolment Rate (NER)

Enrolment of the official age group for a given level of education expressed as a percentage of the corresponding population.

1.4.55 Non-formal (educational) qualification

Qualification awarded upon achievement of the learning objectives of an education programme in non-formal education that is not recognised by the relevant national education authorities as being equivalent to a formal qualification.

1.4.56 Numeracy

The ability to add, subtract, multiply and divide. More broadly, it means the knowledge and skills required to effectively manage and respond to mathematical demands posed by diverse situations, involving objects, pictures, numbers, symbols, formulas, diagrams, maps, graphs, tables and text.

1.4.57 Organized

A planned in a pattern or sequence with explicit or implicit aims. It involves a providing agency (person(s) or body) that facilitates a learning environment, and a method of instruction through which communication is organized. Instruction typically involves a teacher or trainer who is engaged in communicating and guiding knowledge and skills with a view to bringing about learning. The medium of instruction can also be indirect, e.g. through radio, television, computer software, film, recordings, Internet or other communication technologies.

1.4.58 Out-of-School Rate

Children and young people in the official age range for a given level of education who are not enrolled in primary, secondary or higher levels of education.

1.4.59 Participation rate in organized learning (one year before the official primary entry age)

This is an indicator that measures the exposure of children to organized learning activities one year before they start primary school.

1.4.60 Post-Secondary Non-Tertiary Education

Provides learning experiences building on secondary education, preparing for labour market entry as well as tertiary education. It aims at the individual acquisition of knowledge, skills and competencies lower than the level of complexity characteristic of tertiary education.

1.4.61 Primary Education

Provides learning for children of age 6-11 years. Primary education is typically designed to provide students with fundamental skills in reading, writing and mathematics (i.e. literacy and numeracy) and establish a solid foundation for learning and understanding core areas of knowledge, personal and social development, in preparation for lower secondary education. It focuses on learning at a basic level of complexity with little, if any, specialisation.

1.4.62 Pupil-classroom Ratio

Average number of primary school pupils per primary school classrooms.

1.4.63 Pupil-stance Ratio

The number of pupils in the school divided by the total number of latrine stances in the school.

1.4.64 Pupil-Teacher Ratio

Total number of pupils enrolled in a given school divided by the total number of teachers in the same school.

1.4.65 Pupil-textbook Ratio

The number of pupils using a single textbook.

1.4.66 Regular School

This refers to regular or mainstream schools that primarily serve typically developing Learners without significant learning disabilities or special needs. These schools follow the standard curriculum and may provide additional support to Learners who need some assistance, but they do not have specialized programs for Learners with special needs.

1.4.67 Regular Schools with special needs units

These are regular schools that have special education units or resource rooms within the school premises to cater for learners with special needs and disabilities. The said units provide targeted support and services to learners with special needs and disabilities while still allowing them to participate in some general education classes with their peers without special needs and disabilities.

1.4.68 Scholastic Progression (Promotion Rate)

It is a measure of the movement of learners from one grade or level of education to another and is usually a crude measure of prospects or opportunities for mobility that exist within the education system.

1.4.69 Scholastic Retardation (Over-Aged)

It is a measure of persons enrolled in a certain education level when they are already over-age in comparison to the official age category.

1.4.70 School-age Population

The number of children in the population who have attained the official school attendance age based on the national curriculum, whether enrolled in school or not.

1.4.71 Secondary Education

Provides learning for children of age 14-17 and on completion, students sit and receive a Kenya Certificate of Secondary Education (KCSE) qualification. lower secondary education, are typically designed to build on the learning outcomes from primary. Usually, the aim is to lay the foundation for lifelong learning and human development upon which education systems may then expand further educational opportunities. Upper secondary education is typically designed to complete secondary education in preparation for tertiary education or provide skills relevant to employment, or both.

1.4.72 Special needs education

Education is designed to facilitate learning by individuals who, for a wide variety of reasons, require additional support and adaptive pedagogical methods in order to participate and meet learning objectives in an education programme. Reasons may include (but are not limited to) disadvantages in physical, behavioural, intellectual, emotional and social capacities. Education programmes in special needs education may follow a similar curriculum as that offered in the parallel regular education system, but they take individual needs into account by providing specific resources (e.g. specially-trained personnel, equipment or space) and, if appropriate, modified educational content or learning objectives. These programmes can be offered to individual students within already-existing education programmes or as a separate class in the same or separate educational institutions.

1.4.73 Special Schools (special needs only)

These are schools specifically designed to exclusively cater for learners with special needs or significant learning disabilities. These schools have specialized programs, teaching methods, and support services to address the individual needs of their learners. The curriculum provided in these schools may be adapted to suit the abilities and learning styles of their learners.

1.4.74 Students Cohort

A group of pupils who enter the first grade of a level of education in the same school year and subsequently experience promotion, repetition, drop-out or successful completion, each in his or her own way.

1.4.75 Sustained

The learning experience has the elements of duration and continuity.

1.4.76 Teachers

Persons who, in their professional capacity, guide and direct learners in gaining knowledge, attitudes and skills that are stipulated by a defined curriculum programme.

1.4.77 Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET)

Education that offers various technical and vocational courses which include trade test courses in vocational training centres (formerly youth polytechnics), artisan, craft and diploma courses in technical and vocational colleges (formerly technical training institutes and institutes of technology); craft and diploma courses in national polytechnics, the leads to trade tests, certificates and diplomas in various disciplines. This level of education is designed for learners who have completed primary or secondary education and also youth who, for some reason, do not enrol in the regular education system at the primary education level.

1.4.78 Tertiary Education

Tertiary education builds on secondary education, providing learning activities in specialised fields of education. It aims at learning at a high level of complexity and specialisation.

1.4.79 Total Enrolment Rate

Total number of students of the official age group for a given level of education who are enrolled in any level of education, expressed as a percentage of the corresponding population.

1.4.80 Training

Education designed to achieve particular learning objectives, especially in vocational education.

1.4.81 University Education

Is open to students who have completed secondary education, lasts a minimum of 4 years, and leads to a bachelor’s degree. This level of education also includes post-graduate programmes leading to post-graduate diplomas, masters and doctorate degrees.

1.4.82 Vocational education

Education programmes that are designed for learners to acquire the knowledge, skills and competencies specific to a particular occupation, trade, or class of occupations or trades. Vocational education may have work-based components (e.g. apprenticeships, dual-system education programmes). Successful completion of such programmes leads to labour market-relevant vocational qualifications acknowledged as occupationally-oriented by the relevant national authorities and/or the labour market.

1.4.83 Work-based education

Educational activities which take place in a work environment, usually in the context of vocational education programmes. The aim is to achieve specific learning objectives through practical instruction and participation in work activities under the guidance of experienced workers or trainers.

1.4.84 Youth Population

Refers to population age 15-34 for training and age 15-24 as used in enrolment for international comparison.

1.4.85 Transition rate

Transition rate refers to the number of students admitted to the first grade of a higher level of education in a given year, expressed as a percentage of the number of students enrolled in the final grade of the lower level of education in the previous year.

1.5 Health

This section covers health and mortality related statistical activities. The terms defined below are used in computing statistics and indicators on health and mortality:

1.5.1 Activity limitation

Population age who reports being limited in selected activities (home, school, work and other) because of a physical condition, mental condition, or health problem which has lasted or is expected to last six months or longer.

1.5.2 Adult Mortality Rate

This is the probability that a 15-year-old person will die before reaching age 60, expressed per 1,000 population.

1.5.3 Age-Specific Death Rate (ASDR)

This is the number of deaths in a specific age group per 1,000 or 100,000 persons in that same age group during a given year.

1.5.4 Age-specific Mortality Rate

This is the total number of deaths in a specified age group, per 1,000 (or 100,000) persons in that age group in the population over a given time period.

1.5.5 Alcohol and Drug Use Disorders

Alcohol and drug use disorders are mental and behavioral disorders resulting from the harmful or hazardous use of alcohol and psychoactive substances, including illicit drugs and prescription medications.

1.5.6 Antenatal Attendance Coverage

The number of new clients attending antenatal clinics as a proportion of the total number of estimated pregnancies in the service population.

1.5.7 Antenatal Care (ANC) from a Skilled Health Provider

The percentage of women aged 15–49 years with a live birth in a given time period who received antenatal care at least once during pregnancy by a skilled health provider.

1.5.8 Births Attended by Skilled Health Personnel

The proportion of live births attended by skilled health personnel (doctor, nurse, or midwife) trained in providing lifesaving obstetric care.

1.5.9 Birth Weight

This is the first weight of a newborn taken after birth, preferably within the first hour of life before significant postnatal weight loss has occurred.

1.5.10 Body Mass Index (BMI)

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a numerical value of a person’s weight in relation to their height. It’s calculated by dividing weight in kilograms by the square of height in meters (kg/m²). BMI is a tool for classifying underweight, overweight, and obesity in adults.

1.5.11 Breastfeeding Practices

Breastfeeding practices involve initiating breastfeeding within the first hour of birth, exclusively breastfeeding for the first six months of life and continuing breastfeeding alongside complementary foods up to two years of age or beyond.

1.5.12 Case Fatality Rate

Usually expressed as the percentage of persons diagnosed as having a specified disease who die as a result of that illness within a given period.

1.5.13 Causes of Death

All diseases, morbid conditions, or injuries that either resulted in or contributed to death and the circumstances of the accident or violence that produced any such injuries. Symptoms or modes of dying, such as heart failure or asthenia, are not considered to be causes of death for vital statistics purposes.

1.5.14 Chemoprophylaxis

The administration of a medication to prevent disease or infection. It is used before, during or after potential exposure to a pathogen, depending on the disease and risk level.

1.5.15 Child Mortality

Child mortality refers to the death of a child under the age of 5 years.

1.5.16 Childhood Mortality

Childhood mortality refers to the death of a child between birth and adolescence, often disaggregated into specific age-based mortality rates like neonatal, infant, post-neonatal, under-five, and child mortality (1–4 years).

1.5.17 Chronic Pain

This is pain that persists or recurs for longer than 3 months. Severity of pain is measured as severe, moderate or mild.

1.5.18 Communicable Disease

A communicable disease (also called an infectious disease) is a disease that is transmitted from one person to another through a variety of ways including contact with blood and bodily fluids; breathing in an airborne virus; or by being bitten by an insect.

1.5.19 Community Health Promoters

These are personnel who are chosen by community members or organizations to deliver basic health education and services at the household or community level.

1.5.20 Contraceptive Prevalence Rate

Contraceptive prevalence rate is the percentage of women of reproductive age (15–49 years), who are married or in a union, that are currently using any form of contraception, either modern or traditional methods, at a given point in time.

1.5.21 Crude Death Rate

This is the number of deaths occurring during the year per 1,000 population, estimated at mid-year.

1.5.22 Dietary Practices

This is a person’s food consumption habits and preferences. These practices encompass choices about what to eat, how much to eat, when to eat, and how food is prepared and shared.

1.5.23 Disability

Loss or limitation of opportunities to take part in everyday life on equal level with others due to impairment.

1.5.24 Doctor-Patient Ratio

This refers to the number of doctors available to serve a given population.

1.5.25 Epidemic

The occurrence of an illness, specific health related behaviour or other health related events clearly in excess of normal expectancy in a given community or region.

1.5.26 Frequency of Drinking

Frequency of drinking refers to how often a person consumes any type of alcoholic drink, such as beer, wine, spirits, or traditional brews, over a given period (e.g., week, month, or year).

1.5.27 Gestational Age

Gestational age of a newborn or dead foetus is the elapsed time measured from the first day of the last menstrual period of the mother to the date of delivery expressed in completed days or weeks.

1.5.28 Health

A state of complete physical, social and mental well-being, and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity.

1.5.29 Health Care System

Health care system (or health system) refers to all the activities whose primary purpose is to promote, restore, or maintain health.

1.5.30 Health Determinants

Health determinants refer to the range of personal, social, economic and environmental factors that influence health status.

1.5.31 Health Expenditures

The sum of money spent on health care goods and services, including preventive and curative services, family planning activities, nutrition activities and emergency aid designated for health, excluding provision of water and sanitation.

1.5.32 Health Expenditures as a Percent of Gross Domestic Product

Level of current health expenditure expressed as a percentage of GDP. Estimates of current health expenditures include healthcare goods and services consumed during each year. This indicator does not include capital health expenditures such as buildings, machinery, IT and stocks of vaccines for emergency or outbreaks.

1.5.33 Health Expenditures Per Capita

This is the total amount of money spent on health care per person in a country. It’s calculated by dividing the total amount spent on health by the total population. It reflects the average health spending per person in a given year

1.5.34 Health Expenditure as a Percent of Total Government Spending

The ratio of general government expenditure on health (GGHE) to total government expenditure.

1.5.35 Health Facility

A health facility is any location where health care is provided. This includes hospitals, clinics, outpatient care centres, and specialized care centres.

1.5.36 Health Facility Census

A Health facility census is a complete count and assessment of all health facilities in a country or region at a given point in time. It is comprehensive and aims to capture information about every facility.

1.5.37 Health Facility Survey

A Health facility survey is a sample-based or targeted assessment designed to collect in-depth information on the quality, availability, and readiness of services provided by health facilities.

1.5.38 Health Impact Assessment

This is a combination of procedures, methods and tools by which a policy, programme, product or service may be judged concerning its effects on the health of the population and the distribution of those effects within the population.

1.5.39 Health Information System

A health information system is an integrated effort to collect, process, report and use health information and knowledge to influence policy-making, programme action and research.

1.5.40 Health insurance

Health insurance is a mechanism for financing health services through prepayment of regular contributions, with pooling of funds and risk across a group of people, to protect them from catastrophic health spending.

1.5.41 Health Needs Assessment

A systematic approach to ensuring that health service provision is based on a clear understanding of health needs in a given population.

1.5.42 Health Outcomes

Health Outcomes refer to measurable changes in health resulting from actions or inactions in health systems or broader social and economic factors. Health outcomes include both clinical results (e.g. recovery, survival, disease progression) and public health indicators (e.g. life expectancy, mortality rates, quality of life).

1.5.43 Health Policy

Health policy refers to decisions, plans, and actions that are undertaken to achieve specific health care goals within a society.

1.5.44 Health Record

A health record is a repository of information about a person’s health status and healthcare, created and maintained for the purposes of care, communication, legal documentation, research, and planning.

1.5.45 Health Status

Health status is the state of health of an individual, group, or population as assessed by objective measures (such as morbidity, mortality, and disability rates) and subjective perceptions (such as self-reported health status and quality of life).

1.5.46 Health Survey

A health survey is a systematic method of collecting data on health and health-related behaviours, conditions, and services in a population through structured questionnaires or interviews.

1.5.47 Illegal drugs

These are psychoactive substances that are prohibited under international drug control treaties and national laws due to their potential to cause harm and dependency.

1.5.48 Immunization Coverage

Immunization coverage is the proportion of the population that has been vaccinated against a specific disease, according to the recommended national immunization schedule.

1.5.49 Impairment

Any loss or abnormality of psychological or anatomical structure of a function.

1.5.50 Incidence

Incidence measures the appearance of new infections among the part of the population hitherto not infected.

1.5.51 Incidence Rate

A new event or case of a disease (or a death or other health condition) that occurred in a specified time period.

1.5.52 Infant Mortality

Infant mortality refers to the death of a child before reaching one year of age, regardless of the cause.

1.5.53 Infant Mortality Rate (IMR)

The Infant Mortality Rate is the number of deaths of children under one year of age per 1,000 live births in a given year.

1.5.54 Legal Drugs

These are substances that are permitted by law for production, sale, possession, and use, typically for medical, therapeutic, or recreational purposes. These drugs are usually regulated by government authorities.

1.5.55 Life Expectancy

The average number of additional years a person could expect to live if current mortality trends were to continue for the rest of that person’s life. Most commonly cited as life expectancy at birth.

1.5.56 Life expectancy at Age X

The average number of years that a person of X years old could expect to live, if he or she were to pass through life exposed to the sex- and age-specific death rates prevailing at the time of his or her age X.

1.5.57 Life Expectancy at Birth

The average number of years that a newborn could expect to live, if he or she were to pass through life exposed to the sex- and age-specific death rates prevailing at the time of his or her birth, for a specific year, in a given country, territory, or geographic area.

1.5.58 Life Table

A tabular display of life expectancy and the probability of dying at each age (or age group) for a given population, according to the age-specific death rates prevailing at that time.

1.5.59 Master Facility List

The Master facility list is a complete and current list of all health facilities in a country, along with their geographic location and key attributes, used to support planning, service delivery, and information systems.

1.5.60 Maternal Death

The death of a woman while pregnant or within 42 days of termination of pregnancy, irrespective of the duration and site of the pregnancy, from any cause related to or aggravated by the pregnancy or its management but not from accidental or incidental causes.

1.5.61 Maternal Mortality Rate (MMRate)

This is the number of maternal deaths in a given period per 100,000 women of reproductive age (usually 15–49 years) during the same time period.

1.5.62 Maternal Mortality Ratio (MMR)

This is the number of maternal deaths during a given time period per 100,000 live births during the same time period.

1.5.63 Medical Laboratory

A medical laboratory is a facility equipped for performing diagnostic tests on specimens derived from the human body to provide information for the diagnosis, treatment, and prevention of disease in patients.

1.5.64 Morbidity

Morbidity refers to the state of being diseased or unhealthy within a population. It includes both the incidence (new cases) and prevalence (existing cases) of diseases and health conditions.

1.5.65 Morbidity Incidence Rate

The morbidity incidence rate is the number of new cases of a specific disease or health condition that occur in a defined population during a specified period, usually expressed per 1,000 or 100,000 population.

1.5.66 Morbidity Prevalence Rate

Morbidity prevalence rate is the proportion of a population that is affected by a specific disease or health condition at a given point in time (point prevalence) or over a defined period (period prevalence).

1.5.67 Mortality

Mortality refers to the incidence of death in a population during a specific period. There are several measures of mortality, namely childhood mortality, adult mortality (including maternal) and life expectancy at specific ages.

1.5.68 Neonatal Mortality Rate

It is the number of deaths among live-born infants during the first 28 completed days of life per 1,000 live births in a given year or period.

1.5.69 Other Conditions

This refers to a group of illnesses, disorders, or health events that do not fall under the main defined categories.

1.5.70 Outpatient Morbidity

Outpatient morbidity refers to the pattern and frequency of diseases or health conditions diagnosed or reported among patients receiving care without being admitted to a hospital.

1.5.71 Patient Bed Days

Patient bed days (also referred to as inpatient bed days) represent the aggregate number of days during which hospital beds are occupied by admitted patients over a defined period (usually monthly, quarterly, or annually).

1.5.72 Bed Occupancy Rate

Bed occupancy rate is the percentage of available inpatient beds occupied over a given period.

1.5.73 Perinatal Mortality Rate

This refers to the number of stillbirths plus early neonatal deaths (within the first 7 days of life) per 1,000 total births (live births + stillbirths).

1.5.74 Post neonatal Mortality Rate

This refers to the number of deaths of infants aged 28 days to under 1 year of age, per 1,000 live births in a given year.

1.5.75 Preterm Baby

A preterm baby is a baby born alive before 37 completed weeks of pregnancy (before 259 days of gestation), counted from the first day of the last menstrual period.

1.5.76 Primary Care Hospitals

These types of hospitals provide essential healthcare services. They primarily focus on general health needs, preventive care, and treatment of common illnesses.

1.5.77 Secondary Care Hospitals

These hospitals offer specialized outpatient and inpatient services beyond primary care but are less specialized than tertiary care.

1.5.78 Smoking Initiation

Smoking initiation refers to the age at which a person first begins using cigarettes or other tobacco products.

1.5.79 Smoking Status

Smoking status refers to a person’s current or past use of tobacco. WHO classifies smoking status as current smoker, former smoker and never smoker.

1.5.80 Suicide Rate

Suicide rate is the number of deaths resulting from intentional self-inflicted harm per 100,000 people in a specific year.

1.5.81 Teenage Pregnancy

Teenage pregnancy refers to a pregnancy occurring in a female adolescent aged 10 to 19 years.

1.5.82 Tertiary Referral Hospitals

These hospitals provide advanced levels of healthcare services that typically require highly specialized medical professionals, facilities, and equipment.

1.5.83 Tetanus Toxoid (TT) Coverage

Tetanus Toxoid (TT) Coverage is the percentage of women of reproductive age (15–49 years) who have received the recommended doses of tetanus toxoid-containing vaccine (TTCV) to safeguard against maternal and neonatal tetanus. (MNT).

1.5.84 Under-5 Mortality (U5M)

This refers to all deaths of children from birth up to their 5th birthday, regardless of cause.

1.5.85 Under-5 Mortality Rate (U5MR)

This is the probability (expressed per 1,000 live births) that a newborn baby will die before reaching the age of 5 years, if subject to current age-specific mortality rates.

1.5.86 Underlying Cause of Death

The underlying cause of death is the disease or injury that initiated the chain of events leading directly to death, or the circumstances of the accident or violence that produced the fatal injury.

1.5.87 Unintentional Injury

These areinjuries that are not inflicted deliberately and include events such as road traffic injuries, falls, drowning, burns, and poisoning.

1.5.88 Universal Health Coverage (UHC)

Universal Health Coverage means that all individuals and communities receive the health services they need without suffering financial hardship. ## Income and consumption

Households have personal needs and wants that are directly satisfied through consumption of goods and services resulting from activities that are productive in an economic sense. These goods and services are referred to as consumer goods and services and their individual value is defined as the consumption expenditure on these goods or services. 

1.5.89 Household income

Household income is the sum of money income and income in kind and consists of receipts which, as a rule, are of a recurring nature and accrue to the household or to individual members of the household regularly at annual or at more frequent intervals (ILO,2000). Household income consists of receipts in cash, in kind or in services, that are usually recurrent and regular and are received by the household or by individual members of the household at annual or at more frequent intervals. During the reference period when they are received, such receipts are potentially available for current consumption and, as a rule, do not reduce the net worth of the household (ILO,2001). Income is thus defined in terms of a broad grouping of sources of income as follows:

1.5.90 Income from employment

Income from employment comprises receipts from involvement in economic activities, strictly in an employment-related capacity. It consists of employee income (e.g. wages) and self-employment income (return to labour), (ILO, 2000).

1.5.91 Employee income

Employee income consists of direct wages and salaries, cash bonuses and gratuities, commissions, directors’ fees, profit-sharing bonuses and other forms of profit-related pay, remuneration for time not worked (e.g. as sick leave or annual leave), free or subsidized goods and services from an employer as well as severance and termination pay. Employee income can be in cash (in the sense of monetary), in kind as goods or services.

1.5.92 Income from self employment

Income from self-employment is defined as the income which is received, over a given reference period, by individuals, for themselves or in respect of their family members, as a result of their current or former involvement in self-employment jobs” (ILO 2000). Income from self employment is taken as income received as a result of involvement in a self-employment job. It should be noted that this component is restricted to owners of unincorporated enterprises who work in such enterprises.

1.5.93 Property Income

Property incomeis income received from ownership of financial and other assets, e.g. interest payments. Property income consists of receipts received as a result of ownership of financial and non-financial assets that are provided to others for their use. These are usually in the form of interest receipts, dividends, rents received for the use of unproduced assets (natural resources), royalties for use of intellectual property and rents received for produced assets.

1.5.94 Income from household production of services for own consumption

This is income from household production of services for own consumption, e.g. services of owner-occupied housing, household production of domestic services for own consumption. These are services produced within the household for the household’s own consumption and not for the market. They include services from owner-occupied dwellings and from consumer durable goods owned as well as own-produced domestic services. They are valued net of expenses that go into their production

1.5.95 Transfers

Transfers are receipts for which there is no “quid pro quo”, i.e. nothing tangible is given by the recipient to the donor in return for the receipts. Transfers may be made between households, between households and government or between households and charities. Transfers are mainly  motivated by the intention to redistribute income. Transfers can consist of cash (monetary) or goods or of services and can cross national boundaries. Capital transfers are one-off or irregular and are usually large, lump-sum receipts. In contrast, current transfers usually recur regularly and tend to be small. Current transfers are also mostly available for use in consumption expenditure during the reference period while recipients of capital transfers do not usually regard them as income and do not use them wholly for consumption expenditure during the reference period.

1.5.96 Current Transfers (in cash and goods)

Current transfers whether in cash or goods consist of social security pensions/schemes; pensions and other insurance benefits; current transfers from non-profit institutions and current transfers from other households.

1.5.97 Current Transfers (Services)

These aresocial transfers in kind from government-provided services to individual households such as education, health, social welfare, transport and cultural services.

1.5.98 Holding gains

Holding gains are changes in the value of financial and non-financial assets and liabilities over a reference period which occur without any direct action of the owner, for example, changes in the value of stocks and shares. A holding gain, the result of an increase in assets or a reduction in liability, increases the net worth of the owner’s assets while a loss has the opposite effect.

1.5.99 Household Consumption Expenditure

This is the value of consumer goods and services that were acquired (used or paid for) by a household for the direct satisfaction of the needs and wants of its members through direct monetary purchases in the market; through the market-place but without using any money as means of payment (barter, income in kind); or from production within the household (own-account production).

1.5.100 Consumption expenditure on non-durable goods and services

This is expenditure incurred on goods including food items, personal care items, fuel, educational and entertainment items,  household items such as cleaning products and so on.

1.5.101 Consumption expenditure on durable goods

This is expenditure on household kitchen appliances, household information and entertainment appliances, other household appliances, household transportation equipment, other household items, clothing items, utensils, and so on. Consumer goods that are used many times over a long period of time without reducing their capacity to satisfy needs and wants are referred to as durable goods.

1.5.102 Intertemporal consumption

Intertemporal consumption refers to how individuals allocate their consumption of goods and services across different time periods, typically balancing consumption today versus in the future. The decision is influenced by factors such as income, interest rates, time preferences, and expectations about the future.

1.6 Social Protection

Social protection is the systematic intervention intended to relieve households and individuals of the burden of a defined set of social risks. Social protection can be organized as social assistance or social insurance schemes, with the latter organized as social security schemes or employment-related social insurance schemes.” Kenya’s social protection sector is structured into three pillars: social assistance, social security and social health insurance.

1.6.1 Cash transfers

These are direct, regular and predictable transfers that raise and smooth incomes to reduce poverty and vulnerability. Unconditional Cash Transfers (UCTs) are for the beneficiary to decide how to spend. Conditional Cash Transfers (CCTs) are given with the requirement that the beneficiary meets certain conditions – often related to human capital development, such as visiting a health clinic or ensuring children go to school.

1.6.2 Emergency, Chronic, and Critical Illness Fund

This Fund was established under Section 28 of the SHIA (2023), and it covers costs related to managing chronic illnesses after the depletion of social health insurance cover and funds emergency treatment. In contrast, NHIF provides general health insurance coverage for various medical needs. The Emergency, Chronic, and Critical Illness Fund has diverse funding sources, including appropriations by the National Assembly, gifts, grants, donations, endowments, or funds from any other lawful source.

1.6.3 Inua Jamii 70 years and above cash transfer programme (IJ70+)

Inua Jamii is a cash transfer programme implemented by the Kenyan government to cushion vulnerable members of society and improve their livelihood. The objective of the program is to uplift the lives of poor and vulnerable Kenyans through regular and reliable cash transfers. It represents the first building block towards a universal social protection system in Kenya. The programme is completely funded by the Government and aims to reach all people aged 70 or older who are not already in receipt of a civil service pension, providing them with a monthly cash transfer disbursed every two months.

1.6.4 National Safety Net Programme (NSNP)

This is a programme that provides a common operating framework to harmonise the delivery of Kenya’s tax-financed social protection schemes.

1.6.5 National Social Security Fund

This is a mandatory national scheme whose main objective is to provide basic financial security benefits to Kenyans upon retirement. The Fund was set up as a Provident Fund providing benefits in the form of a lump sum.

1.6.6 Primary Healthcare Fund

This is a fund that focuses on procuring primary healthcare services from health facilities. It is funded through various sources, including appropriations by the National Assembly, grants, gifts, donations, bequests, fees, levies, and funds from other relevant sources. SHI aims to remove limitations on specific medical expenses, such as pre-treatment blood works and certain CT scans, and includes coverage for congenital conditions and preventive measures not previously covered.

1.6.7 Public works programmes (PWPs)

These are programmes that provide jobs on infrastructure projects for cash or food. They are sometimes classified as labour market interventions depending on whether their function is primarily poverty alleviation, job creation, or social protection. In Kenya, such programmes include ‘Kazi Kwa Vijana’.

1.6.8 Retirement Benefits Authority (RBA)

This is a regulatory body established by the Government of Kenya to supervise the establishment and management of retirement benefits schemes in the Republic of Kenya.

1.6.9 School feeding

This is a free nutritious meal at school – usually lunch – and sometimes take-home rations for children most in need. This is a type of in-kind assistance. They help encourage parents to keep children in school.

1.6.10 Social assistance

This is direct, regular and predictable cash or in-kind resources transfers to poor and vulnerable individuals or households. It is usually provided by the state and financed by national taxes. Transfers are non-contributory, i.e. the full amount is paid by the provider. Some are targeted based on categories of vulnerability, and some are targeted broadly to low-income groups.

1.6.11 Social Health Authority (SHA)

This is a State corporation under the Government of Kenya responsible for the provision and management of public health insurance within the Republic of Kenya. The core mandate of the SHA is to provide accessible, affordable, sustainable, and quality health insurance for all Kenyan citizens and, where applicable, foreign residents.

1.6.12 Social Health Insurance (SHI)

This is a social health insurance system in Kenya that is operated through individuals contributing funds to a common pool through regular premiums, deducted from salaries or paid directly, to finance care services for themselves and their dependents. The Social Health Insurance Fund is governed by the Social Health Insurance Act (SHIA) of 2023 and it introduces three funds: Social Health Insurance Fund (SHIF), Primary Healthcare Fund and Emergency, Chronic, and Critical Illness Fund.

1.6.13 Social Health Insurance Fund

This is a fund aimed at promoting the implementation of universal health coverage by ensuring all individuals and communities receive the health services they need, including the full spectrum of essential, quality health services from health promotion to prevention, treatment, rehabilitation, and palliative care without suffering financial hardship. The fund has been established by the government to improve the quality of life for all citizens by providing equitable, accessible, affordable, and high-standard healthcare.

1.6.14 Social Insurance

These are contributory programmes where participants make regular payments to a scheme that will cover costs related to life-course events, for example, maternity, unemployment or illness. Social insurance includes contributory pensions; health, unemployment, or disaster insurance; and funeral assistance. Social insurance is strongly linked to the formal labour market, meaning coverage is often limited to formal workers.

1.6.15 Social pensions

These are state pensions, a form of cash transfer targeted by age. Pensions are the most common social protection tool, with the widest global coverage and often highest national spend. The current pensions schemes in Kenya comprise the National Social Security Fund (NSSF) which is a state contributory scheme mandatory for all employers and employees in the formal sector and informal sector workers who can contribute on voluntary basis, Civil Servants Pension Scheme which is non-contributory and administered by The National Treasury for the government for retired civil servants and (iii) private pension schemes.

1.6.16 Social risks

Defined as events or circumstances that may adversely affect the welfare of households either by imposing additional demands on their resources or by reducing their income. Needs may occur due to sickness, unemployment, retirement, housing, education, or family circumstances.

1.6.17 Social Security

Social security is defined as the security that the society furnishes through appropriate organizations against certain risks to which its members are exposed. The concept of social security is based on ideas of human dignity and social justice. The underlying idea behind social security measures is that a citizen who has contributed or is likely to con­tribute to his/her country’s welfare should be given protection against certain hazards. The concept of social security, thus, is based on the ideals of human dignity and socio-economic justice. Underlying the concept is also the desire to give protection to its citizens to contribute to a coun­try’s total welfare against certain hazards of life to which they are exposed either in the working life or as a consequence of it.

1.6.18 The Cash Transfer for Orphans and Vulnerable Children (CT-OVC)

This is a poverty-targeted and household-based programme with the objective of providing regular cash transfers to poor families living with children identified as OVCs to encourage fostering and retention of children and promote their human capital development. OVCs are defined in the programme as children aged 0-17 years old with at least one deceased parent, or a parent who is chronically ill, or whose main caregiver is chronically ill.

1.6.19 The Cash Transfer Persons with Severe Disabilities (CT-PwSD)

This is a poverty-targeted and household-based programme that seeks to enhance the capacities of care givers through cash transfers and as such, improve the livelihoods of persons with severe disabilities as well as reduce negative impact of disability on households. It targets adults and children with severe disabilities, who require full time support of a caregiver. The objectives of the programme are to: to strengthen the capacities of parents and children with disabilities; improve the livelihoods of parents and children with disabilities and to alleviate integrated poverty among parents and children with disabilities.

1.6.20 The Hunger Safety Net Programme (HSNP)

This is a poverty-targeted and household-based programme where cash is transferred unconditionally to households in ASALs with the aim of reducing poverty, food insecurity and malnutrition, and promote asset retention and accumulation in poor households.

1.7 Human settlements and housing

1.7.1 Housing

Refers to the processes and outcomes of the production (construction) and consumption (use) of residential (living) shelters.It includes the physical product, the process that yields the product, and the socio-cultural and environmental circumstances in which the product is developed, delivered and used. It also involves the process of analysing the shelter needs of society, organizing resources and facilitating society to access shelter that is adequate, affordable, functional and environmentally sustainable.

1.7.1.1 Cement

Building material is usually greenish to gray in colour and sometimes light brown. It hardens in moist conditions.

1.7.1.2 Cement concrete

Refers to a mixture of cement, sand, coarse aggregate and water in specified proportions which can be moulded into any shape. It is weak under tensile stress and strong under compression.

1.7.1.3 Consolidated Housing Quality Index (CHQI)

Refers to an indicator of housing quality derived by combining the quality of materials of construction of the structure (roof, wall and floor) and the level of service provision (water, drainage) and energy for cooking and lighting.

1.7.1.4 Cooking fuel

Refers to the source(s) of energy the household uses for cooking such as LPG, electricity, paraffin, charcoal and firewood.

1.7.1.5 Durable floor

Refers to any floor made of wood, terrazzo, concrete, wood tiles, brick, cement, or quarry tiles.

1.7.1.6 Durable roof

Refers to any roof made of tin, tiles, concrete, corrugated iron and asbestos sheets.

1.7.1.7 Durable wall

Refers to any wall made of bricks, blocks, stones and concrete.

1.7.1.8 Fence

An artificially constructed barrier of any material or combination of materials, erected to screen, enclose or separate areas.

1.7.1.9 Habitable room

Any room in a dwelling unit that is used mainly for living and excludes stores, granaries, offices, toilets and garages. In some circumstances, a kitchen or a store which under normal circumstances should not be counted as a habitable room are included, if members of the household used it for eating and/or sleeping purposes or even for purposes of entertaining guests.

A bedroom is defined as a room equipped with a bed and used for night rest.

1.7.1.10 Household amenity

Refers to any facility or service made available to provide comfort or convenience to a household in the occupation and use of a dwelling unit e.g. electricity, sewerage, water.

1.7.1.11 Household asset

Refers to any of the movable property belonging to a particular household, these include radio, TV, car, truck/lorry/tractor/bus, canoe, mobile phone, refrigerator, landline telephone, boat, animal-drawn cart, computer, bicycle, motorcycle and auto-rickshaw (tuk-tuk) etc.

1.7.1.12 Household head

This is the most responsible/respectable member of the household who makes key decisions of the household on a day-to-day basis and whose authority is recognized by all members of the household. It could be the father, the mother or a child, or any other responsible member of the household depending on the status of the household.

1.7.1.13 Household size

The total number of persons living in one household.

1.7.1.14 House

A structure/building designed for residential or non-residential purposes or both and may be occupied or not.

1.7.1.15 Housing need

Refers to the number and type of housing units required to accommodate a population at a given standard of housing occupancy within a designated geographical area.

1.7.1.16 Housing quality

Refers to the adequacy of housing in terms of structural standards and the level of provision of services.

1.7.1.17 Housing Unit/Dwelling Unit

A separate and independent place of abode intended for habitation by a single household, or one not intended for habitation but occupied as living quarters by a household at the time of the census.

1.7.1.18 A Conventional Dwelling

This is a room or suite of rooms and its accessories in a permanent building or structurally separated part thereof, which, by the way it has been built, rebuilt or converted, is intended for habitation by one household.

1.7.1.19 A Permanent Building

A structure that is not intended to be moved and that may be expected to maintain its stability for 15 years or more, depending on the way countries define durability.

1.7.1.20 Mobile Housing Unit

This is any type of living quarter that has been produced to be transported e.g. a tent. A mobile housing unit may also refer to a moving unit such as a ship, a boat, a caravan, trailers, boats, tents, etc. occupied as living quarters at the time of the census.

1.7.1.21 A room

This is a space in a housing unit enclosed by walls reaching from the floor to the ceiling or roof covering, or to a height of at least 2 metres, of an area large enough to hold a bed for an adult, that is, at least 4 square metres.

1.7.1.22 Non-residential building

A building where more than 50% of floor space area is not intended for dwelling but for other purposes such as commercial or industrial or for provision of services i.e. factories, office building, government buildings, garages, warehouses, barns etc. It also covers buildings for persons living in institutions, such as hospital buildings, prisons, military establishments and so forth.

1.7.1.23 Permanent Dwelling Units

These are built with durable materials (wall, floor and roof) that can maintain their stability for at least 15 years.

1.7.1.24 Piped water

Refers to water that is usually supplied to households through pipes. The can either be connected from the source to the individual’s stand/plot or from the source to the individual’s housing unit.

1.7.1.25 Piped water into dwelling

Also called a household connection, is defined as a water service pipe connected with in-house plumbing to one or more taps (e.g., in the kitchen and bathroom).

1.7.1.26 Piped water to yard/plot (yard connection)

A piped water connection to a tap placed in the yard or plot outside the house.

1.7.1.27 A protected Dug well

A dug well that is protected from runoff water by a well lining or casing that is raised above ground level and a platform that diverts spilled water away from the well.

1.7.1.28 A tubewell or borehole

A deep hole that has been driven, bored or drilled, with the purpose of reaching groundwater supplies.

1.7.1.29 Rainwater

Rain that is collected or harvested from surfaces (by roof or ground catchment) and stored in a container, tank or cistern until used.

1.7.1.30 Unprotected Spring

This is a spring that is subject to runoff, bird droppings, or the entry of animals.

1.7.1.31 Unprotected dug well

This is a dug well for which one of the following conditions is true: 1) the well is not protected from runoff water; or 2) the well is not protected from bird droppings and animals. If at least one of these conditions is true, the well is unprotected.

1.7.1.32 Cart with small tank/drum

This refers to water sold by a provider who transports water into a community. The types of transportation used include donkey carts, motorized vehicles and other means.

1.7.1.33 Rivers or ponds

This refers to surface water is water located above ground and includes rivers, dams, lakes, ponds, streams, canals, and irrigation channels.

1.7.1.34 Protected Spring

The spring is typically protected from runoff, bird droppings and animals by a “spring box”, which is constructed of brick, masonry, or concrete and is built around the spring so that water flows directly out of the box into a pipe or cistern, without being exposed to outside pollution.

1.7.1.35 Tanker-truck

The water is trucked into a community and sold from the water truck.

1.7.2 Rent

The charge for use of land, buildings or other real property, payable by a tenant to a landlord at regular intervals, usually monthly.

1.7.2.1 Residential building

A building for which the major part (more than 50% of its gross floor area) is built for dwelling purposes, i.e. for habitation of households.

1.7.2.2 Room

A space in a housing unit or other living quarters enclosed by walls reaching from the floor to the ceiling or roof covering, or at least to a height of 2 metres, of a size large enough to hold a bed for an adult, that is at least 4 square metres. Passage ways, verandahs, lobbies, bath-rooms, and toilet rooms are not expected to be counted as rooms, even if they meet the criteria.

1.7.2.3 Source of water

The point at which a household accesses water for domestic use.

1.7.2.4 Tenant or Renter

An occupant of land or building in subordination to owner’s title and with the owner’s assent, who holds real estate under lease, who pays rent in return for the right to occupy land or building, who holds or possesses land or building by title.

1.7.2.5 Tenure

The proprietary status indicates whether households are owners or tenants in their dwellings.

1.7.2.6 Toilet

 An installation for the disposal of human excreta.

1.7.2.7 Wall

A vertical load-bearing or non-load-bearing member of a structure whose length exceeds four times its thickness.

1.7.3 Human Settlement Terms

1.7.3.1 Crowding

A condition where household occupancy exceeds a dwelling’s capacity for adequate living space, typically measured by persons per room or floor area per person. Overcrowding occurs when more than three persons share a single habitable room.

1.7.3.2 Human waste disposal

Refers to the handling of human excreta including faeces and urine.

1.7.3.3 Informal settlements

Refers to urban settlements characterized by poor structural quality of housing; lack of formal basic services and infrastructure; and lack of security of tenure. In most cases, they are located in geographically and environmentally hazardous areas.

1.7.3.4 Rural area

Refers to a large and isolated part of an open or agricultural country with relatively low population concentrations and whose predominant economic activity is agricultural.

1.7.3.5 Slum

A predominantly residential area characterized by high population densities, deteriorated buildings, littered streets, unsanitary and hazardous conditions and economic distress.

1.7.3.6 Urban area

Refers to any aggregate part of the county, region or country made up of urban centres or parts of urban centres.

1.7.3.7 Urban Centre

This is characterised by a concentration of economic activity and infrastructure, in addition to having a vastly higher population density than its surrounding areas. It might be seen as a built-up and compact human settlement with a population of at least 2,000 people. An urban centre may be classified as a market centre, town, municipality or city. Urban centres are also service centres that provide goods and services to both the resident and surrounding population. As such, an urban centre may constitute some trading centres with less than 2,000 people.

1.7.3.8 Urbanization

This is the process of concentration of a country’s national population into settlements designated as urban centres. It’s the process whereby a society is transformed from an essentially rural to a predominantly urban one.

1.8 Culture

1.8.1 Culture

Culture is a set of distinctive spiritual, material, intellectual and emotional features of society or a social group that encompasses not only art and literature, but lifestyles, ways of living together, value systems, traditions and beliefs (UNESCO, 2001). Whereas it is not always possible to measure such beliefs and values directly, it is possible to measure associated behaviours and practices.

Culture plays a key role in all societies around the world, influencing various facets of peoples’ lives, from leisure to professional activities.

1.8.2 Conservation

Are measures undertaken to extend the life of cultural heritage while strengthening transmission of its significant heritage messages and values (ICCROM, 1998). In the domain of cultural property, the aim of conservation is to maintain the physical and cultural characteristics of the object to ensure that its value is not diminished and that it will outlive our limited time span (UNESCO, 1988).

1.8.3 Copyright

This is one of the main branches of intellectual property. It applies to “every production in the literary, scientific and artistic domain, whatever may be the mode or form of its expression. The expression “literary and artistic works” is to be understood, for the purposes of copyright protection, as including every original work of authorship, irrespective of its literary or artistic merit. Literary and artistic works include books, pamphlets and other writings, music, plays, choreography, photography, films, paintings, drawings, architecture, engraving, lithography (WIPO, 2003, 1979).

1.8.4 Craft or artisanal products

These are defined as “those produced by artisans, either completely by hand or with the help of hand-tools or even mechanical means, as long as the direct manual contribution of the artisan remains the most substantial component of the finished product. The special nature of artisanal products derives from their distinctive features, which can be utilitarian, aesthetic, artistic, creative, culturally attached, decorative, functional, traditional, religiously and socially symbolic and significant” (UNESCO and ITC, 1997).

1.8.5 Cultural activities

They embody or convey cultural expressions, irrespective of the commercial value they may have. Cultural activities may be an end in themselves or they may contribute to the production of cultural goods and services.

1.8.6 Cultural diversity

Refers to the many ways in which the different cultures of groups and societies find expression. These cultural expressions are passed on within and among groups and societies, and from generation to generation. Cultural diversity, however, is evident not only in the varied ways in which cultural heritage is expressed, augmented and transmitted but also in the different modes of artistic creation, production, dissemination, distribution and enjoyment, whatever the means and technologies that are used.

1.8.7 Cultural goods

Defined as consumer goods that convey ideas, symbols and ways of life, i.e. books, magazines, multimedia products software, recordings, films, videos, audio-visual programmes, crafts and fashion.

1.8.8 Cultural heritage

This includes artefacts, monuments, a group of buildings and sites that have a diversity of values including symbolic, historic, artistic, aesthetic, ethnological or anthropological, scientific and social significance (UNESCO, 1972). Cultural industries produce and distribute cultural goods or services as defined above.

1.8.9 Cultural landscape

Represents combined works of nature and humans, and they express a long and intimate relationship between people and their natural environment (UNESCO, 2007).

1.8.10 Cultural participation

This is participation in the arts and everyday life activities that may be associated with a particular culture. It refers to “the ways in which ethnically-marked differences in cultural tastes, values and behaviours inform not just artistic and media preferences but are embedded in the daily rhythms of different ways of life; and of the ways in which these connect with other relevant social characteristics – those of class and gender, for example” (Bennett, 2001).

1.8.11 Cultural services

This is participation in the arts and everyday life activities that may be associated with a particular culture. It refers to “the ways in which ethnically-marked differences in cultural tastes, values and behaviours inform not just artistic and media preferences but are embedded in the daily rhythms of different ways of life; and of the ways in which these connect with other relevant social characteristics – those of class and gender, for example” (Bennett, 2001).

1.8.12 Design

Consists of units mainly engaged in the creative, artistic and aesthetic design of objects, environments and services (ABS, 2001).

1.8.13 Fashion design

The process of conceptualizing and creating clothing, footwear, and fashion accessories that reflect aesthetic, cultural, or functional values. It is recognized as part of the creative industries and may involve both artisanal and industrial methods. Fashion design is protected under intellectual property frameworks such as industrial design and copyright

1.8.14 Graphic design

Carried out by companies or individuals engaged primarily in the design of graphic material for the purposes of display or publication.

1.8.15 Indigenous

Peoples are persons in independent countries who are regarded as “indigenous” on account of their descent from the populations which inhabited the country, or a geographical region to which the country belongs, at the time of conquest or colonisation or the establishment of present state boundaries and who, irrespective of their legal status, retain some or all of their own social, economic, cultural and political institutions” (ILO, 1989).

1.8.16 Intangible cultural heritage (ICH)

Defined as “the practices, representations, expressions, knowledge, skills – as well as the instruments, objects, artefacts and cultural spaces associated therewith – that communities, groups and, in some cases, individuals recognise as part of their cultural heritage. This intangible cultural heritage, transmitted from generation to generation, is constantly recreated by communities and groups in response to their environment, their interaction with nature and their history, and provides them with a sense of identity and continuity, thus promoting respect for cultural diversity and human creativity” (UNESCO, 2003).

1.8.17 Interactive media

Defined as media where either i) two or more objects have an effect on one another; ii) the user can effect a change on an object or within the environment (users playing videos games); iii) they involve active participation of a user; or iv) there is two way effect as opposed to one way or simple cause effect (Canadian Heritage, 2008).

1.8.18 Interior design

Activities carried out by companies and individuals engaged primarily in the design of interior environments of buildings, private and public, residential and commercial (Statistics New Zealand, 1995).

1.8.19 Landscape design

Covers the design of lands and gardens for human use. Usually, this relates to the design of and adjacent to buildings, either private or public, but the design can also relate to large public areas such as parks and reserves (Statistics New Zealand, 1995).

1.8.20 Library

This is an organisation, or part of an organisation, whose main aims are to build and maintain a collection and to facilitate the use of such information resources and facilities as are required to meet the informational, research, educational, cultural or recreational needs of its users; these are the basic requirements for a library and do not exclude any additional resources and services incidental to its main purpose (ISO, 2006). It includes any organized collection of books and periodicals in electronic or in printed form or of any other graphic or audio-visual materials (adapted from UNESCO, 1970). It includes virtual libraries, digital catalogues.

1.8.21 Museum

A non-profit, permanent institution in the service of society and its development, open to the public, which acquires, conserves, researches, communicates and exhibits the tangible and intangible heritage of humanity and its environment for the purposes of education, study and enjoyment.

a. The above definition of a museum shall be applied without any limitation arising from the nature of the governing body, the territorial character, the functional structure or the orientation of the collections of the institution concerned.

b. In addition to institutions designated as ‘museums’ the following qualify as museums for the purposes of this definition:

i. natural, archaeological and ethnographic monuments and sites and historical monuments and sites of a museum nature that acquire, conserve and communicate material evidence of people and their environment;

ii. institutions holding collections of and displaying live specimens of plants and animals, such as  botanical and zoological gardens, aquaria and vivaria;

iii. science centres and planetaria;

iv. non-profit art exhibition galleries

v. nature reserves; conservation institutes and exhibition galleries permanently maintained by libraries and archives centres; natural parks;

vi. international or national or regional or local museum organisations, ministries or departments or public agencies responsible for museums as per the definition given under this article;

vii. non-profit institutions or organisations undertaking conservation research, education, training, documentation and other activities relating to museums and museology;

viii. cultural centres and other entities that facilitate the preservation, continuation

and management of tangible or intangible heritage resources (living heritage and digital creative activity); and

ix. such other institutions as the Executive Council, after seeking the advice of the Advisory Committee, considers as having some or all the characteristics of a museum, or as supporting museums and professional museum personnel through museological research, education or training (ICOM 2007).

1.8.22 Natural heritage

This consists of natural features, geological and physiographical formations and delineated areas that constitute the habitat of threatened species of animals and plants and natural sites of value from the point of view of science, conservation or natural beauty. It includes nature parks and reserves, zoos, aquaria and botanical gardens (UNESCO, 1972).

1.8.23 Performing Arts and Celebration

Refers to professional or amateur performing arts activities, such as theatre, dance, opera and puppetry, as well as the celebration of cultural events (festivals, feasts and fairs), which occur locally and can be informal in nature.

1.8.24 Preservation

The aim of preservation is to prevent damage liable to be caused by environmental or accidental factors, which pose a threat in the immediate surroundings of the object to be conserved. Accordingly, preventive methods and measures are not usually applied directly but are designed to control the microclimatic conditions of the environment with the aim of eradicating harmful agents or elements, which may have a temporary or permanent influence on the deterioration of the object (UNESCO, 1988).

1.8.25 Recreation

Defined as an activity undertaken for pleasure or relaxation that diverts amuses or stimulates.

1.8.26 Sports and Physical recreation

Consists of companies and individuals mainly engaged in the presentation or provision of sports and physical recreation and associated services. Coaching and education services related to sports and physical recreation are included. Also included are business units, which manufacture wholesale or retail sports and physical recreation goods.

1.8.27 Traditional knowledge

Refers to the knowledge, innovations and practices of indigenous and local communities around the world. Developed from experience gained over the centuries and adapted to the local culture and environment, traditional knowledge is transmitted orally from generation to generation. It tends to be collectively owned and takes the form of stories, songs, folklore, proverbs, cultural values, beliefs, rituals, community laws, local language and agricultural practices, including the development of plant species and animal breeds. Traditional knowledge is mainly of a practical nature, particularly in such fields as agriculture, fisheries, health, horticulture, forestry and environmental management in general” (SCBD, 2007).

1.8.28 Tribal peoples

Persons in independent countries whose social, cultural and economic conditions distinguish them from other sections of the national community, and whose status is regulated wholly or partially by their own customs or traditions or by special laws or regulations.

1.8.29 Visual arts

Appeal primarily to the visual sense; they are art forms that focus on the creation of works, which are primarily visual in nature, or are multidimensional objects.

1.9 Time-Use

1.9.1 Average time spent

The average time spent in an activity can be computed either with reference to the whole population of the category or specifically to those who participated in the activity. Therefore, the participation rate for the activity is required, and two series of tables are presented:

1.       Average time spent per day by the population and

2.     Average time spent per day by persons who engaged in the activity.

Basic time use statistics typically provide estimates of the average time spent per day on various types of activities by individuals within specific demographic groups. These categories may include men, women, boys, and girls within certain age groups, as well as distinctions such as rural or urban residence, employment status, and more. To ensure accurate averages, time use data should encompass both weekdays and weekends, and account for seasonal variations throughout the year.

1.9.2 Participation rate

The participation rate refers to the proportion of individuals in the survey population who took part in a specific activity during the reference period, such as in  the last 24 hours.

1.9.3 Time slot

A one-hour interval within a 24-hour period during which respondents reported one or more activities.

1.9.4 Simultaneous activities

Simultaneous activities are those undertaken by the same person at the same time and place. For instance: cooking while listening to the radio, or cleaning the house while caring for the children etc.

1.9.5 System of National Accounts (SNA) Productive

Activities within the SNA production boundary include the production of goods and services supplied or intended to be supplied to others, own-account production of goods retained by producers, own-account production of housing services by owner-occupiers and of domestic and personal services produced in a household by paid domestic staff.

The SNA excludes unpaid household activities that produce domestic or personal services for consumption within the same household, except when performed by paid staff.

1.9.6 Non-SNA productive (extended SNA)

Non-SNA include unpaid domestic and personal services produced and consumed within the same household, such as cleaning, maintenance, and repairs, meal preparation and serving, care and instruction of children, care for the sick, elderly, or infirm, transportation of household members or their goods and unpaid volunteer services to other households, communities, or associations

1.9.7 Non-productive activities

An activity is considered non-productive if it cannot be delegated to someone else, according to the “third-person rule.” These include personal maintenance activities such as eating, sleeping, exercising and leisure or recreational activities such as watching TV, playing games and hobbies.

1.9.8 Productive work

Productive work refers to activities that can be delegated to another person and still produce the same result. All such activities fall within the general production boundary.

1.9.9 Reproductive work

Reproductive work includes activities such as rearing and caring for children; caring for the elderly, ill, disabled and other household members; caring unpaid for non-family members; and cooking, cleaning and fetching water and fuel. Though these activities are productive, they are not included in GDP calculations.

1.9.10 Care of children and adults

This refers to time spent caring for or helping any child (under 18) or adult in the household, regardless of their health status. Care is categorized into:

1.9.11 Primary childcare

Time spent directly and exclusively on a child while not engaged in any other activity, such as feeding, bathing, playing, or helping with homework.

1.9.12 Primary adult care

Time spent directly helping or caring for an adult, such as bathing, grooming, or assisting with household tasks like cleaning or cooking.

1.9.13 Secondary care of children and adults

Care provided while engaged in another primary activity. For example, watching over a child doing homework while cooking dinner or watching over a sick parent while doing house chores

1.9.14 Time-use surveys

Statistical survey that aims to report data on how people spend their time. If the necessary R&D coefficients cannot be derived from other surveys or administrative data, time-use surveys are the recommended method to derive the necessary informationfor estimating the R&D component of full-time equivalents (FTEs) and expenditures.

1.9.15 Passive Care

Monitoring or supervising another person without direct interaction. For example, watching children get dressed or supervising a family member taking medication. This may occur while performing other tasks or as a primary activity.

1.9.16 Active Care

Active care involves direct interaction with the person being cared for. Examples include helping someone dress or administering medication.

1.9.17 Household Production Satellite Account

A statistical framework that estimates the value of unpaid work in the household, such as childcare, adult care, housework, and volunteering. Specifically, the household production satellite accounts show the relation between the monetary value of unpaid work to the total GDP providing relevant information that complements the GDP.

1.9.18 Simultaneous or parallel activities

These involve performing two or more activities during the same time interval. The primary activity is the one whose value added exceeds that of other activities and it’s deemed most important or requiring the most concentration. The secondary activity is carried out at the same time as the primary activity but considered of lesser importance

1.9.19 Time-use diaries

Diaries that capture all activities undertaken by a respondent over a defined reference period (e.g. a full 24-hour period, a weekday and a weekend day, a full week, etc.). They capture start and end times, activity duration, sequence, and frequency.

1.9.20 Activity classification system

A structured system that categorizes activities into groups to analyze how people spend their time. It supports policymaking and statistical reporting through a hierarchical structure that assigns numeric codes to each activity.

1.9.21 International Classification of Activities for Time-Use Statistics (ICATUS):

A standard classification of all activities that the general population may engage in over a 24 hours period. The classification provides categories for compiling internationally comparable time-use statistic
